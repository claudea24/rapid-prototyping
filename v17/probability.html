Probability

3 classic probability puzzles that appear frequently in data science interviews.

Challenge 1 — Bayes' Theorem Medical Test

Medium

Think about it... A disease affects 1 in 1,000 people. A test for the disease has a 99% true positive rate (sensitivity) and a 1% false positive rate. If a person tests positive, what is the probability they actually have the disease?

Using Bayes' Theorem:

P(Disease | Positive) = P(Positive | Disease) * P(Disease) / P(Positive)

Given:
- P(Disease) = 0.001
- P(Positive | Disease) = 0.99 (sensitivity)
- P(Positive | No Disease) = 0.01 (false positive rate)

P(Positive) = P(Pos|D)*P(D) + P(Pos|~D)*P(~D)
= 0.99 * 0.001 + 0.01 * 0.999
= 0.00099 + 0.00999
= 0.01098

P(Disease | Positive) = 0.00099 / 0.01098 = 0.0902 (about 9%)

Key insight: Even with a 99% accurate test, a positive result only means a ~9% chance of having the disease! This is because the disease is so rare (low base rate) that the false positives from the healthy population vastly outnumber the true positives. This is the "base rate fallacy" — one of the most important concepts in probabilistic reasoning.

Challenge 2 — Birthday Problem

Easy

Think about it... How many people do you need in a room for there to be a greater than 50% chance that at least two people share the same birthday? Walk through your reasoning step by step.

Answer: 23 people.

Approach: It's easier to calculate P(no shared birthdays) and subtract from 1.

P(all different) for n people:
= (365/365) * (364/365) * (363/365) * ... * ((365-n+1)/365)
= 365! / ((365-n)! * 365^n)

For n = 23:
P(all different) = 365/365 * 364/365 * 363/365 * ... * 343/365
= 0.4927...

P(at least one match) = 1 - 0.4927 = 0.5073 (50.7%)

Why it's counterintuitive: People think about the chance of someone sharing THEIR birthday (which needs ~253 people for 50%). But the birthday problem asks about ANY pair — and with 23 people, there are C(23,2) = 253 possible pairs. The combinatorial explosion of pairs is what makes the threshold so low.

At 50 people, the probability rises to 97%. At 70 people, it's 99.9%.

Challenge 3 — Monty Hall

Hard

Think about it... You're on a game show with 3 doors. Behind one door is a car; behind the others, goats. You pick door 1. The host (who knows what's behind the doors) opens door 3, revealing a goat. Should you switch to door 2 or stick with door 1? Prove your answer with probabilities.

You should ALWAYS switch. Switching wins 2/3 of the time.

Proof by enumeration:

Assume you always pick Door 1:

Case 1: Car behind Door 1 (prob 1/3)
- Host opens Door 2 or 3. Switch = LOSE.

Case 2: Car behind Door 2 (prob 1/3)
- Host must open Door 3 (the only goat). Switch to Door 2 = WIN.

Case 3: Car behind Door 3 (prob 1/3)
- Host must open Door 2 (the only goat). Switch to Door 3 = WIN.

Switching wins in 2 out of 3 cases = 2/3 probability.
Staying wins in 1 out of 3 cases = 1/3 probability.

Using Bayes' Theorem:
P(Car=2 | Host opens 3) = P(Host opens 3 | Car=2) * P(Car=2) / P(Host opens 3)
= 1 * (1/3) / (1/2) = 2/3

Key insight: Your initial pick has 1/3 chance. The host's action doesn't change that — it concentrates the remaining 2/3 probability onto the single unopened door. The host's knowledge (always reveals a goat) is what creates the asymmetry.
