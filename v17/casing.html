Case Studies

3 real-world case study problems covering metric diagnosis, market sizing, and experiment design. Each challenge has its own timer.

Challenge 1 — Diagnose the Metric Drop

Medium
Time:00:00

Think about it... You're a DS at an e-commerce company. The CEO messages you: "Daily revenue dropped 15% yesterday compared to the same day last week. What happened?" Walk through your diagnostic framework step by step. What data would you look at? What are the most likely causes?

Diagnostic Framework:

Step 1: Validate the data
- Is the data pipeline working correctly? Check for ETL failures, logging issues, or tracking bugs.
- Is this a data issue or a real business issue?

Step 2: Decompose the metric
Revenue = Traffic * Conversion Rate * AOV (Average Order Value)
- Which component dropped? Check each one independently.

Step 3: Segment the drop
- By platform: Mobile vs Desktop vs App
- By geography: Any regional issues?
- By traffic source: Organic vs Paid vs Direct vs Email
- By product category: Is it across the board or specific?
- By user type: New vs Returning customers

Step 4: Check external factors
- Was there a holiday, competitor sale, or major news event?
- Weather events affecting a key region?
- Payment processor outage?

Step 5: Check internal changes
- Any code deployments yesterday? (site bugs, checkout issues)
- Marketing campaigns paused or budgets changed?
- Price changes or promotions ending?
- A/B tests launched that might affect conversion?

Most likely causes (ordered by frequency):
1. Technical bug (broken checkout, tracking failure)
2. Marketing budget/campaign change
3. External event (competitor promotion, holiday shift)
4. Seasonal pattern (compare YoY, not just WoW)

Challenge 2 — Market Size Estimation

Easy
Time:00:00

Think about it... Estimate the annual revenue of all coffee shops in the United States. Show your reasoning with a top-down or bottom-up approach (or both). State your assumptions clearly.

Bottom-Up Approach:

Assumptions:
- US population: ~330 million
- Adults (18+): ~260 million
- % who drink coffee: ~65% = ~170 million coffee drinkers
- % who buy from shops (not just home): ~40% = ~68 million
- Average purchases per week: 3
- Average spend per visit: $5.50
- Weeks per year: 52

Calculation:
68M people * 3 visits/week * $5.50/visit * 52 weeks
= 68M * 858/year
= ~$58 billion/year

Top-Down Sanity Check:
- ~35,000 Starbucks in US, avg revenue ~$1.5M/year = $52.5B (just Starbucks)
- There are ~38,000 other coffee shops
- Total shops: ~73,000, average revenue maybe $800K
= ~$58 billion

Both approaches converge around $55-60 billion.
(Actual US coffee shop market: ~$50-60B, so this estimate is reasonable.)

Key to good estimation: State assumptions clearly, use round numbers, and sanity-check with a different method.

Challenge 3 — Design the Experiment

Hard
Time:00:00

Think about it... A ride-sharing company wants to test a new surge pricing algorithm. The old algorithm raises prices 2x during peak hours; the new one uses ML to set dynamic prices between 1.2x-3x based on demand signals. How would you design this experiment? Consider: randomization unit, metrics, duration, risks, and ethical concerns.

Experiment Design:

Randomization unit: Geographic zones (not individual users)
- Why not users? A rider in Zone A seeing 1.5x while a rider next to them sees 2.5x creates fairness issues and marketplace interference (drivers will move toward higher-priced zones, contaminating the experiment).
- Use "switchback" design: alternate algorithms across time blocks (e.g., hour-by-hour) within zones to control for zone-level confounders.

Primary metrics:
- Revenue per ride-request
- Ride completion rate (do more rides actually happen?)
- ETA (are enough drivers available?)

Guardrail metrics:
- Rider cancellation rate
- Driver earnings per hour
- Rider NPS / satisfaction score
- Price fairness complaints

Duration: At least 4 weeks to capture weekday/weekend cycles, holidays, and various demand patterns. Need sufficient surge events to have statistical power.

Risks:
1. Revenue loss if new algorithm underprices consistently
2. Driver supply imbalance between zones
3. PR risk if extreme prices (3x) go viral
4. Network effects: pricing in one zone affects driver supply in adjacent zones

Ethical concerns:
- Price discrimination: Does the ML model inadvertently charge more in lower-income areas?
- Transparency: Do riders know why prices vary?
- Emergency situations: Should surge pricing be capped during emergencies?
- Set a maximum price cap (e.g., 2.5x) for the experiment to limit downside risk.

Analysis plan: Use difference-in-differences with zone and time fixed effects. Cluster standard errors at the zone level.
