Probability

Rules, distributions, and Bayes' theorem

Fundamentals

Key
  • Addition rule: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B)
  • Multiplication rule: P(A and B) = P(A) · P(B|A)
  • Independence: A and B are independent if P(A and B) = P(A) · P(B)
  • Complement: P(A') = 1 − P(A)
  • Permutations: n! / (n − r)! — order matters
  • Combinations: n! / [r!(n − r)!] — order doesn't matter

Bayes' Theorem

Key
P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B)

Prior × Likelihood / Evidence = Posterior

Classic disease test example: A test is 99% sensitive and 99% specific. If disease prevalence is 1%, what's the probability someone who tests positive actually has it?

Using Bayes: P(Disease|+) = (0.99 × 0.01) / [(0.99 × 0.01) + (0.01 × 0.99)] = 50%. The low base rate dramatically lowers the positive predictive value.

Expected Value & Variance

Key
E[X] = ∑ x·P(x)
Var(X) = E[X²] − (E[X])²
  • Linearity of expectation: E[X + Y] = E[X] + E[Y], always — even when X and Y are dependent
  • Variance of sum: Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y) only if X and Y are independent
  • Scaling: E[aX] = a·E[X], Var(aX) = a²·Var(X)

Law of Large Numbers

Key

As the number of trials n increases, the sample mean converges to the expected value. This is why casinos always win in the long run and why larger samples produce more reliable estimates.

  • Weak LLN: Sample mean converges in probability
  • Strong LLN: Sample mean converges almost surely
  • Key requirement: Observations must be i.i.d. with finite mean
  • Common confusion: LLN does not say outcomes "balance out" — it says the average stabilizes

Classic Interview Puzzles

Tip
  • Birthday problem: Only 23 people needed for a >50% chance two share a birthday. The trick: calculate the complement (all different).
  • Monty Hall: Always switch! Staying = 1/3, switching = 2/3. The host's reveal gives you new information.
  • Coupon collector: Expected draws to collect all n types = n · H(n), where H(n) is the n-th harmonic number. Grows as n·ln(n).
  • Dice problem: Expected rolls to see all 6 faces = 6 · (1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/4 + 1/5 + 1/6) ≈ 14.7
Practice Questions
Q1: In the Monty Hall problem, should you switch doors?
Correct! When you initially pick, you have a 1/3 chance. The host always reveals a goat, so switching gives you the remaining 2/3 probability. The key insight is that the host's action is not random — they always reveal a losing door.
Q2: How many people do you need in a room for a greater than 50% chance that two share a birthday?
Correct! With just 23 people, the probability of at least one shared birthday exceeds 50%. This feels counterintuitive because we compare all pairs — there are C(23,2) = 253 possible pairs, which is a lot of chances for a match.
