F1

Product Metrics (AARRR)

+50 XP ▼

The "pirate metrics" framework captures the user lifecycle in five stages:

StageDefinitionExample MetricsWhy It Matters
AcquisitionHow users discover productSign-ups, CPA, channel splitCan't grow without awareness
ActivationFirst core value experienceOnboarding %, time to first actionMultiplies acquisition value
RetentionDo users come back?D1/D7/D30, churn, cohort curvesMost important; compounds
RevenueHow product makes moneyARPU, LTV, conversion, MRRValidates business model
ReferralDo users bring others?NPS, viral coefficientFree, pre-qualified acquisition

The North Star Metric — a single number that captures core value your product delivers:

CompanyNorth StarWhy It Works
AirbnbNights bookedValue for both guests and hosts
SpotifyTime spent listeningEngagement proxy for satisfaction
FacebookDaily active usersHabitual use → monetization
Key Insight
A good North Star aligns user value with business value. If it grows while users are unhappy, it's the wrong metric.
F2

Structuring Your Approach

+75 XP ▼

The six-step framework for any case question:

1. Clarify — Restate the problem. Ask scope questions.
2. Define Metrics — Primary metric + guardrail metrics.
3. Hypothesize — State leading hypotheses before touching data.
4. Analyze — Segment by cohort, platform, geography, time.
5. Recommend — Concrete action + expected impact estimate.
6. Measure — How will you validate? What experiment?

Example: "WAU Dropped 15%"

Clarify: Week-over-week? All platforms?
Metrics: Primary = WAU. Guardrails = revenue/user, session quality.
Hypothesize: (a) Product bug, (b) seasonal, (c) marketing campaign ended.
Analyze: Segment by platform → drop is iOS only. By version → v4.7. Check crashes → spike after deploy.
Recommend: "iOS v4.7 crash bug. Immediate hotfix + re-engagement push. Recovers ~12 of 15 lost points within one week."
Measure: Track iOS WAU recovery daily. Monitor crash rates and App Store ratings.

F3

Diagnosing Metric Changes

+75 XP ▼

Investigation Framework:

1. Data problem? Check logging pipeline, schema changes, processing delays.
2. External factor? Holidays, competitor launches, weather, news events.
3. Internal change? New feature, UI redesign, pricing change, experiment ramp.
4. Segment to isolate. Platform, geography, cohort, device, subscription tier.
5. Quantify the contribution. Does the identified driver explain 90% or 20%?

Example: "Revenue Up 20% but Rides Down 5%"

Revenue = Rides × Revenue per Ride
1.20 = 0.95 × X
X ≈ 1.263 → Revenue per ride up ~26%

Hypotheses: Price increase? Mix shift to longer/premium rides? New surge algorithm? Segment by ride type, geography, time of day to find the primary driver.

F4

Market Sizing

+100 XP ▼
ApproachStart WithStrength
Top-DownLarge known numberFast, macro-level
Bottom-UpSingle unitMore grounded, concrete

Example: Piano Tuners in Chicago

Step 1 — Pianos: 9.5M population → 3.5M households → 5% own piano = 175K + 10K institutional = 185K pianos
Step 2 — Tunings/year: ~1 per piano = 185K tunings
Step 3 — Tunings per tuner: 2 hrs each, 2000 hrs/year = 1000 tunings/tuner/year
Step 4 — Divide: 185K / 1000 = ~185 piano tuners (actual: 100–300)

Interactive: Random Fermi Question

Key Insight
Interviewers care about structure and assumptions, not the exact number. A clearly reasoned estimate off by 2x is infinitely better than a lucky guess.
F5

Experiment Design

+100 XP ▼
DecisionDefaultWatch Out
Randomization unitUser-levelNetwork effects → cluster randomization
Duration≥ 1 full weekly cycleDay-of-week effects, pay cycles
GuardrailsAlways include severalMust not degrade even if primary improves
Network effectsStandard (if independent)Social/marketplace → cluster
Novelty/primacyRun 2–4 weeksInitial lift may fade; resistance may resolve

Example: Testing New Checkout (4 → 2 steps)

Unit: User-level. Primary: Checkout completion rate. Guardrails: AOV, 14-day return rate, support contacts.

Sample size: Current 65%, detect 2pt lift, α=0.05, power=0.80. Need ~4,400/arm. At 2,000/day = 5 days needed. Round to 14 days (two weekly cycles + novelty fade).

F6

Communicating Your Analysis

+75 XP ▼

Lead with the answer (Pyramid Principle). State the conclusion first, then supporting evidence. "The drop was caused by an iOS v4.7 bug. Here's how I found that."

Signal your structure. "I'll approach this in three parts: clarify the metric, investigate causes, then recommend an action."

Quantify everything. Not "affected a lot of users" but "affected approximately 120,000 users, ~8% of MAU."

Acknowledge trade-offs. "Rolling out the hotfix recovers engagement but delays the redesign by 2 sprints." Naming trade-offs strengthens credibility.

Ask clarifying questions. "When you say engagement, do you mean DAU, time spent, or actions per session?"

Key Insight
The best technical answer, poorly communicated, loses to a decent answer well communicated. Analysis only creates value if someone understands it well enough to act on it.
