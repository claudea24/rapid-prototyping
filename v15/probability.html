F2

Bayes' Theorem

+100 XP ▼ 
P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B)

Posterior = (Likelihood × Prior) / Evidence

The Medical Test Problem: Disease affects 1% of the population. Test is 99% accurate. You test positive. What's P(disease)?

Intuition says 99%. Reality: ~50%.

P(+) = P(+|D)·P(D) + P(+|D')·P(D')
     = 0.99 × 0.01 + 0.01 × 0.99
     = 0.0198

P(D|+) = 0.0099 / 0.0198 = 0.50 (50%)

Why so low? In 10,000 people: 100 sick (99 test +), 9,900 healthy (99 falsely test +). Total positives: 198. Only 99 are diseased — exactly half. The base rate (1% prevalence) dominates.

F4

Expected Value & Variance

+75 XP ▼ 
E[X] = Σ x · P(X = x)       -- long-run average
Var(X) = E[X²] − (E[X])²  -- spread around mean

Linearity:  E[aX + bY] = a·E[X] + b·E[Y]
            (works even when X, Y are dependent!)

Variance:   Var(aX + b) = a² · Var(X)
            If independent: Var(X+Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y)

Example: Pay $5 to roll a die. Roll 6 → win $20. Roll 4 or 5 → win $10. Otherwise $0.

E[winnings] = (1/6)·20 + (2/6)·10 + (3/6)·0 − 5
            = 3.33 + 3.33 + 0 − 5 = $1.67

Expected profit is positive — you should play this game.

F5

Law of Large Numbers

+50 XP ▼

As sample size n → ∞, the sample mean converges to the population mean μ. More data = more reliable estimates.

Why this matters:

• A/B testing backbone: The observed conversion rate converges to the true rate with enough data.
• Small samples are noisy. Large samples are reliable. This is why experiments run for weeks, not hours.
• "We tried it once and it didn't work" is not a statistically valid conclusion.

Key Insight
LLN says sample means converge to the true mean. The CLT tells us how — normally, with SE = σ/√n. Together they form the foundation of statistical inference.
