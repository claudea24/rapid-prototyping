5.2 Bayes' Theorem

▾ 
P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B)

Posterior = (Likelihood × Prior) / Evidence
Worked Example — The Medical Test

Disease prevalence: 1%. Test accuracy: 99% (sensitivity and specificity). You test positive.

  • P(+) = 0.99 × 0.01 + 0.01 × 0.99 = 0.0198
  • P(D|+) = 0.0099 / 0.0198 = 50%

Consider 10,000 people: 100 sick (99 test positive), 9,900 healthy (99 false positives). Total positives: 198. Only half actually have the disease. The base rate dominates.

5.4 Expected Value & Variance

▾ 
E[X] = Σ x · P(X = x)          // Long-run average
Var(X) = E[X²] − (E[X])²

// Linearity of Expectation (works even for dependent variables!)
E[aX + bY] = a · E[X] + b · E[Y]
Worked Example — Dice Game

Pay $5 to roll a die. Roll 6 → win $20. Roll 4 or 5 → win $10. Otherwise → $0.

E[winnings] = (1/6) × 20 + (2/6) × 10 + (3/6) × 0 − 5 = $1.67 profit. You should play.

5.5 Law of Large Numbers

▾

As sample size n → ∞, the sample mean converges to the population mean μ. More data = more reliable estimates. This is the backbone of A/B testing — the observed conversion rate converges to the true rate with enough data.

Key Insight

"We tried it once and it didn't work" is not a statistically valid conclusion. Small samples are noisy. Large samples are reliable. This is why experiments run for weeks, not hours.

5.6 Classic Interview Puzzles

▾
The Birthday Problem

How many people for a ≥50% chance two share a birthday? 23 people.

Use complementary counting: P(all unique) = (365/365) × (364/365) × ... At n=23, P(match) crosses 50%. Why? 23 people = C(23,2) = 253 pairs, each with 1/365 chance.

The Monty Hall Problem

3 doors, 1 car, 2 goats. You pick a door. Host reveals a goat behind another door. Always switch — wins 2/3 of the time.

Your initial pick has 1/3 chance. The other two doors collectively have 2/3. When the host reveals a goat, all 2/3 concentrates on the remaining door.

The Coupon Collector Problem

How many boxes to collect all n distinct coupons? E[T] = n · H(n) ≈ n · (ln(n) + 0.577). For n=50: ~225 boxes.
