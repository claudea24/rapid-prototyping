6.2 Structuring Your Approach

▾
1
Clarify
Restate the problem. Ask scope questions: which product? platform? segment? time window?
2
Define Metrics
Primary metric + guardrail metrics to prevent unintended harm
3
Hypothesize
State leading hypotheses before touching data
4
Analyze
Segment by cohort, platform, geography, time, feature, traffic source
5
Recommend
Concrete action + expected impact estimate
6
Measure
How will you validate? What experiment? Which metric?
Worked Example — "WAU Dropped 15% Last Week"

Clarify: 15% WoW? All platforms? Hypothesize: (a) Product bug, (b) Seasonal/external, (c) Marketing ended. Analyze: Segment by platform → drop is iOS only → app version v4.7 → crash spike. Recommend: Hotfix + re-engagement push. Estimate recovery of ~12 of 15 lost points.

6.3 Diagnosing Metric Changes

▾

Investigation framework: (1) Data problem? Check logging pipeline. (2) External factor? Holidays, competitors, news. (3) Internal change? Feature release, UI redesign, pricing. (4) Segment to isolate. (5) Quantify the contribution.

Worked Example — "Revenue Up 20% but Rides Down 5%"

Decomposition: Revenue = Rides × Revenue per Ride. 1.20 = 0.95 × X → Revenue per ride up ~26%.

Hypotheses: Price increase? Mix shift toward longer/premium rides? New surge pricing? Next: segment by ride type, geography, time of day.

6.4 Market Sizing

▾
ApproachStart WithStructureStrength
Top-DownLarge known numberTAM → filter → targetFast, macro-level
Bottom-UpSingle unitUnit economics → scaleMore grounded, concrete
Worked Example — Piano Tuners in Chicago
  • Chicago metro: ~3.5M households, ~5% own a piano = 175,000 + 10,000 institutional = 185,000 pianos
  • ~1 tuning/year = 185,000 tunings/year
  • Tuner: 8hr/day × 5 days × 50 weeks = 2,000 hrs, 2 hrs/tuning = 1,000 tunings/year
  • 185,000 / 1,000 = ~185 piano tuners (actual: 100–300)
Key Insight

Interviewers care about structure and assumptions, not the exact number. A clearly reasoned estimate off by 2x beats a lucky guess with no justification.

Worked Example — New Checkout Flow (4 → 2 Steps)

Unit: User-level. Primary: Checkout completion rate. Guardrails: AOV, 14-day return rate, support contacts. Sample size: Detect 2-point lift (65% → 67%), α=0.05, power=0.80. Need ~4,400/arm. At 2,000 users/day = round to 14 days (2 weekly cycles + novelty fade).

6.6 Communicating Your Analysis

▾

Pyramid Principle: Lead with the answer, then supporting evidence. "The drop was caused by an iOS v4.7 bug. Here's how I found that."

Signal your structure: "I'll approach this in three parts: clarify the metric, investigate causes, recommend an action." Quantify everything: Not "affected a lot of users" but "affected ~120,000 users, ~8% of MAU." Acknowledge trade-offs: "Rolling out the hotfix recovers engagement but delays the redesign by 2 sprints."

Key Insight

The best technical answer, poorly communicated, loses to a decent answer well communicated. Analysis only creates value if someone understands it well enough to act on it.
