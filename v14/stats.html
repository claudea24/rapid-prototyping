4.2 Hypothesis Testing

▾

The framework: (1) Formulate H₀ and H₁, (2) Choose α (usually 0.05), (3) Collect data, (4) Compute test statistic, (5) Compare to critical value or compute p-value, (6) Reject or fail to reject H₀.

The p-value is the probability of observing data at least as extreme as what you got, assuming H₀ is true. It does NOT mean the probability that H₀ is true.

ErrorNameAnalogyProbability
Type IFalse PositiveConvicting an innocent personα (usually 0.05)
Type IIFalse NegativeAcquitting a guilty personβ
PowerCorrect RejectionCatching the guilty1 − β (target ≥ 0.80)
Worked Example — Testing a New Feature

Baseline conversion: 12%. H₀: conversion = 12%. H₁: conversion > 12%. Sample: 2,000 users, observed 13.8%. z = 2.48, p = 0.0066. Since p < 0.05, reject H₀ — the new flow significantly increases conversions.

4.4 Confidence Intervals

▾ 
CI = x̄ ± z* × (σ / √n)

z* = 1.645 (90%)  |  1.96 (95%)  |  2.576 (99%)

Correct interpretation: If we repeated the sampling procedure 100 times, approximately 95 of those intervals would contain the true parameter. Confidence is in the procedure, not the single interval.

FactorEffect on WidthCan You Control?
Sample size nLarger → narrower (via √n)Yes — run bigger study
Variability σMore → widerNo (inherent to data)
Confidence level99% wider than 95%Yes (trade-off with precision)

4.5 A/B Testing

▾

The full process: (1) Define metric, (2) Power analysis for sample size, (3) Randomize, (4) Run for fixed duration, (5) Analyze, (6) Measure impact.

PitfallWhat Goes WrongThe Fix
PeekingChecking daily inflates FP rate from 5% to ~30%Decide duration in advance; use sequential testing
Multiple comparisons10 metrics → ~40% chance of spurious significanceBonferroni correction: α/number of tests
Novelty effectLift fades as newness wears offRun long enough for novelty to fade
Simpson's paradoxOverall positive masks negative in subgroupsSegment by key dimensions
Key Insight

The hardest part of A/B testing isn't the statistics — it's defining the right metric. Spend as much time debating the metric as analyzing results.

4.6 The Central Limit Theorem

▾

Take any population with finite mean μ and standard deviation σ. Draw random samples of size n. As n grows, the distribution of sample means approaches a normal distribution with mean = μ and SE = σ / √n.

The Intuition: Roll a single die — uniform distribution. Roll 30 dice and average — bell curve centered at 3.5. Works for any starting distribution. Rule of thumb: n ≥ 30 is usually sufficient.

Key Insight

The Law of Large Numbers tells us sample means converge to the true mean. The CLT tells us how — normally, with SE = σ/√n. Together, they form the foundation of statistical inference.
