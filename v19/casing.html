Case Studies — Company Interview Patterns

Meta leads in metric diagnosis and experiment design. Amazon focuses on business metrics. Google tests market sizing.

6.1 Product Metrics (AARRR) MetaAmazon

▾
Meta and Amazon test AARRR framework knowledge. Expect questions about North Star metrics and funnel analysis.

The "pirate metrics" framework captures the user lifecycle in five stages: Acquisition (how users discover), Activation (first core value experience), Retention (do they come back?), Revenue (how it makes money), Referral (do they bring others?).

StageDefinitionExample MetricsWhy It Matters
AcquisitionHow users discover productSign-ups, CPA, channel splitCan't grow without awareness
ActivationFirst core value experienceOnboarding %, time to first actionMultiplies acquisition value
RetentionDo users come back?D1/D7/D30, churn, cohort curvesMost important; compounds
RevenueHow product makes moneyARPU, LTV, conversion, MRRValidates business model
ReferralDo users bring others?NPS, viral coefficientFree, pre-qualified acquisition
Key Insight

A good North Star Metric aligns user value with business value. If it grows while users are unhappy or the business is losing money, it's the wrong metric.

6.2 Structuring Your Approach MetaGoogleAmazon

▾
All companies value structured thinking. The six-step framework works universally.

The six-step framework: (1) Clarify — restate the problem, ask scope questions. (2) Define Metrics — primary metric + guardrails. (3) Hypothesize — state leading hypotheses before touching data. (4) Analyze — segment along relevant dimensions. (5) Recommend — concrete action + expected impact. (6) Measure — how will you validate?

Example — "WAU Dropped 15%": Clarify (which platform? vs. prior week or same week last year?). Define (WAU primary, revenue per user guardrail). Hypothesize (product bug, seasonal, marketing ended). Analyze (segment by platform → iOS only → v4.7 crash bug). Recommend (hotfix + re-engagement push, recovers ~12 of 15 points). Measure (track iOS WAU daily).

Key Insight

Showing structured thinking matters more than getting the exact right answer. Signal your structure: "I'll approach this in three parts..."

6.3 Diagnosing Metric Changes MetaAmazon

▾
This is Meta's #1 case study topic. Amazon also tests metric investigation skills frequently.

Investigation framework: (1) Is it a data problem? Check logging pipeline. (2) Is it an external factor? Holidays, competitors, weather. (3) Is it an internal change? New release, UI redesign, pricing. (4) Segment to isolate: platform, geography, cohort, device. (5) Quantify the contribution: does the identified driver explain 90% or 20%?

Example: "Revenue Up 20% but Rides Down 5%"

Revenue = Rides × Revenue per Ride
1.20 = 0.95 × X
X ≈ 1.263 → Revenue per ride up ~26%

Hypotheses: price increase, mix shift to longer rides,
or premium tier launch driving higher average fare.
Key Insight

Data problems are embarrassingly common — rule them out first. A schema migration breaking tracking is more likely than a genuine business change.

6.4 Market Sizing GoogleAmazon

▾
Google and Amazon both test market sizing. Google loves the classic "how many X in Y?" format.

Top-Down: Start with a large known number, narrow through filters. TAM → segment → penetration → target. Bottom-Up: Start with a single unit, scale up. Unit economics → units per location → number of locations → total.

ApproachStart WithStructureStrength
Top-DownLarge known numberTAM → filter → targetFast, macro-level
Bottom-UpSingle unitUnit economics → scaleMore grounded, concrete

Example — Piano Tuners in Chicago: 9.5M metro → 3.5M households → 5% own piano = 175K + 10K institutions = 185K pianos. 1 tuning/year = 185K tunings. 1 tuner does ~1,000/year. Answer: ~185 tuners. (Actual: 100-300.)

Key Insight

Interviewers care about structure and assumptions, not the exact number. A clearly reasoned estimate off by 2x is infinitely better than a lucky guess.

▾
Meta and Amazon test experiment design extensively. Expect questions about randomization, guardrails, and network effects.

Randomization unitUser, session, pageUser-levelNetwork effects → cluster

Network effectsStandard vs. clusterStandard (if independent)Social/marketplace products
Novelty/primacyShort vs. long testRun 2-4 weeksInitial lift may fade

Example — New Checkout Flow (4 → 2 steps): Unit: user-level. Primary: checkout completion rate. Guardrails: AOV, 14-day return rate, support contacts. Current: 65%, detect 2-point lift, α=0.05, power=0.80. Need ~4,400/arm. At 2,000/day = 5 days needed, round up to 14 days.

Key Insight

Guardrail metrics protect against unintended harm. Even if your primary metric improves, confirm you haven't degraded user experience elsewhere.

6.6 Communicating Your Analysis MetaGoogleAmazon

▾
All companies include a behavioral/communication component. How you present matters as much as what you find.

Lead with the answer (Pyramid Principle). State the conclusion first, then supporting evidence. Signal your structure: "I'll approach this in three parts." Quantify everything: Not "affected a lot of users" but "affected ~120,000 users, ~8% of MAU."

Acknowledge trade-offs: No recommendation is without costs. "Rolling out the hotfix recovers engagement but delays the redesign by 2 sprints." Ask clarifying questions: "When you say engagement, do you mean DAU, time spent, or actions per session?"

Key Insight

The best technical answer, poorly communicated, loses to a decent answer well communicated. Analysis only creates value if someone understands it well enough to act on it.
