Probability — Company Interview Patterns

Google is the #1 probability interview company. They test Bayes, distributions, and classic puzzles extensively.

5.1 Fundamentals Google

▾
Google is the #1 probability interview company. Expect core rules, counting, and complement problems.

Addition Rule: P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A ∩ B). Multiplication Rule: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B|A). Independence: P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B). Complement: P(A) = 1 - P(A’).

5.2 Bayes' Theorem GoogleMeta

▾
Google and Meta both test Bayes' theorem. The medical test problem is a classic that appears frequently.

Bayes' Theorem: P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B). In words: Posterior = (Likelihood × Prior) / Evidence.

The Medical Test Problem: A disease affects 1% of the population. A test is 99% accurate (99% sensitivity, 99% specificity). You test positive. What's the probability you have the disease?

P(+) = P(+|D) · P(D) + P(+|D') · P(D')
     = 0.99 × 0.01 + 0.01 × 0.99
     = 0.0198

P(D|+) = P(+|D) · P(D) / P(+)
       = 0.0099 / 0.0198
       = 0.50 (50%)

Why so low? Consider 10,000 people: 100 are sick (99 test positive), 9,900 are healthy (99 falsely test positive). Total positives: 198. Only 99 are actually diseased — exactly half. The base rate (1% prevalence) dominates.

Key Insight

Always consider the base rate. A 99% accurate test is not very useful when the condition is rare. This is the most counter-intuitive result in probability.

5.4 Expected Value & Variance GoogleAmazon

▾
Google and Amazon test expected value problems. Linearity of expectation is the key tool for solving them efficiently.

Expected Value: E[X] = Σ x · P(X = x). The long-run average. Variance: Var(X) = E[X²] - (E[X])².

Linearity of Expectation: E[aX + bY] = a·E[X] + b·E[Y]. This works even when X and Y are dependent! Incredibly powerful for decomposing complex expected values.

Pay $5 to roll a fair die.
Roll 6 → win $20. Roll 4 or 5 → win $10. Otherwise → $0.

E[winnings] = (1/6) · 20 + (2/6) · 10 + (3/6) · 0 - 5
            = 3.33 + 3.33 + 0 - 5
            = $1.67  ← positive, you should play
Key Insight

Linearity of expectation lets you decompose complex problems into simple pieces without worrying about dependencies. It's the most powerful tool for expected value interview questions.

5.5 Law of Large Numbers Meta

▾
Meta tests LLN in the context of A/B testing. Why do experiments need to run for weeks, not hours?

As sample size n → ∞, the sample mean X̄ converges to the population mean μ. More data = more reliable estimates.

Why this matters for data science: The observed conversion rate in an A/B test is a sample mean. LLN guarantees it converges to the true rate with enough data. Small samples are noisy; large samples are reliable. "We tried it once and it didn't work" is not statistically valid.

Key Insight

LLN tells us that sample means converge to the true mean. The CLT tells us how — normally, with SE = σ/√n. Together, they form the foundation of statistical inference.

5.6 Classic Interview Puzzles Google

▾
Google loves brain teasers and probability puzzles. These test decomposition and careful conditional reasoning.

The Birthday Problem: How many people needed for ≥50% chance two share a birthday? Answer: 23. Use complementary counting: P(all unique) = (365/365) × (364/365) × ... At n=23, P(at least one match) crosses 50%. Why? 23 people = C(23,2) = 253 pairs to compare.

The Monty Hall Problem: 3 doors, 1 car, 2 goats. You pick a door. Host opens another showing a goat. Should you switch? Yes — switching wins 2/3 of the time. Your initial pick has 1/3 chance. The other two doors collectively have 2/3. When host reveals a goat, all 2/3 concentrates on the remaining door.

ScenarioStaySwitch
Car behind your door (1/3)WinLose
Car behind door 2 (1/3)LoseWin
Car behind door 3 (1/3)LoseWin

Coupon Collector: E[T] = n · H(n) where H(n) = harmonic number ≈ ln(n) + 0.5772. For n=50, E[T] ≈ 225 boxes.

Key Insight

These puzzles test problem decomposition, correct conditional probability, and the ability to check whether an answer passes the smell test. When unsure, try a small example first.
