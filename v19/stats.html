Statistics — Company Interview Patterns

Meta leads in A/B testing and hypothesis testing. Google demands methodological rigor. Amazon focuses on confidence intervals and business experiments.

4.1 Descriptive Statistics MetaGoogleAmazon

▾
All companies expect you to know when to use mean vs. median and how to detect outliers.

The mean is sensitive to outliers. The median is robust. Always ask "is my data skewed?" to decide which to use. IQR (Q3 - Q1) measures the middle 50% and is robust to outliers.

MeasureWhat It Tells YouSensitive ToWhen to Use
MeanAverage valueOutliers, skewSymmetric data
MedianMiddle valueNothing (robust)Skewed data, outliers
ModeMost frequent—Categorical data
VarianceSpread (squared units)OutliersMathematical properties
Std DevSpread (same units)OutliersReporting spread
IQRMiddle 50% spreadNothing (robust)Outlier detection
Key Insight

Always ask "is my data skewed?" If yes or outliers suspected, prefer median and IQR over mean and standard deviation. Using the wrong summary statistic is common, quiet damage.

4.2 Hypothesis Testing Meta

▾
Meta has a heavy A/B testing culture. Expect detailed questions about hypothesis formulation, p-values, and error types.

The framework: (1) Formulate H0 and H1, (2) Choose significance level alpha, (3) Collect data, (4) Compute test statistic, (5) Compare to critical value or compute p-value, (6) Conclude.

The p-value is the probability of observing data at least as extreme as what you got, assuming H0 is true. It does NOT mean the probability that H0 is true.

ErrorNameAnalogyProbability
Type IFalse PositiveConvicting an innocent personα (usually 0.05)
Type IIFalse NegativeAcquitting a guilty personβ
PowerCorrect RejectionCatching the guilty1 - β (target ≥ 0.80)
Key Insight

A small p-value says: "data this extreme would be very unlikely if H0 were true." It does not tell you how likely H0 is. This distinction trips up many candidates.

▾

Decision flowchart: Is outcome categorical? → Chi-squared. Continuous, how many groups? One → one-sample t-test. Two → paired or independent t-test. Three+ → ANOVA. Can't assume normality? → Mann-Whitney U.

Key Insight

Always check assumptions before applying a test. Using a t-test on heavily skewed data without verifying normality is a common interview mistake.

4.4 Confidence Intervals MetaAmazon

▾
Meta and Amazon test confidence interval interpretation. The "95% probability" misconception is a common trap.

Formula: CI = x̄ ± z* × (σ / √n). A 95% CI does NOT mean "95% probability the true parameter is in this interval." Correct: if we repeated the procedure 100 times, approximately 95 intervals would contain the true parameter.

FactorEffect on WidthCan You Control?
Sample size nLarger → narrower (via √n)Yes — run bigger study
Variability σMore → widerNo (inherent to data)
Confidence level99% wider than 95%Yes (trade-off with precision)
Key Insight

Confidence is in the procedure, not the single interval. This is the most commonly misunderstood concept in statistics interviews.

4.5 A/B Testing MetaAmazon

▾
This is Meta's #1 topic. Amazon also tests experiment design heavily. Expect peeking, multiple comparisons, and novelty effect questions.

The full process: (1) Define metric, (2) Power analysis for sample size, (3) Randomize (user-level), (4) Run for fixed duration, (5) Analyze at predetermined end, (6) Document how to validate.

PitfallWhat Goes WrongThe Fix
PeekingFP rate inflates from 5% to ~30%Decide duration in advance; use sequential testing
Multiple comparisons10 metrics → ~40% chance of false positiveBonferroni: α / number of tests
Novelty effectUsers engage more just because it's newRun long enough for novelty to fade
Simpson's paradoxOverall positive masks negative subgroupsAlways segment by key dimensions
Key Insight

The hardest part of A/B testing isn't the statistics — it's defining the right metric. Spend as much time debating the metric as analyzing results.

4.6 Central Limit Theorem Google

▾
Google tests CLT understanding deeply. Expect to explain why confidence intervals work and what happens with small samples.

Take any population with finite mean μ and standard deviation σ. Draw random samples of size n. As n grows, the distribution of sample means approaches a normal distribution with Mean = μ and Standard Error = σ / √n.

The 68-95-99.7 rule: ~68% within 1σ, ~95% within 2σ, ~99.7% within 3σ. Rule of thumb: n ≥ 30 is usually sufficient, though more skewed populations need larger samples.

Why it matters: Confidence intervals use x̄ ± z*·SE, which relies on x̄ being approximately normal. P-values compare test statistics to the standard normal. Without CLT, we would need the exact population distribution before making any inference.

Key Insight

The Law of Large Numbers tells us that sample means converge to the true mean. The CLT tells us how — normally, with SE = σ/√n. Together, they form the foundation of statistical inference.
