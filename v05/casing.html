Product Metrics

North Star metrics, AARRR funnel, and metric change frameworks

⭐ North Star Metric

A North Star Metric (NSM) is the single metric that best captures the core value your product delivers to customers. It aligns teams, guides priorities, and predicts long-term sustainable growth.

Examples:
Facebook → Daily Active Users (DAU)
Airbnb → Nights Booked
Spotify → Time Spent Listening
Slack → Messages Sent per Team/Day

A good NSM should be: measurable, reflect customer value, be a leading indicator of revenue, and be actionable by the team.

📈 AARRR Pirate Funnel

The AARRR framework (coined by Dave McClure) breaks customer lifecycle into five stages:

🌎 Acquisition — How do users find you?
⚡ Activation — Do they have a great first experience?
🔁 Retention — Do they come back?
💰 Revenue — Do they pay?
📣 Referral — Do they tell others?

Each stage has its own set of metrics. In an interview, identify which stage is most relevant before diving into metrics.

🔍 Metric Change Questions

When asked "Metric X dropped 10%", use this 5-step framework:

1Clarify the metric — How is it defined? What time period? Any recent changes to tracking?
2Check external factors — Seasonality, holidays, competitor actions, market shifts.
3Segment the data — Break down by platform, geography, user cohort, new vs. returning.
4Identify root cause — Look for correlated changes: deploys, feature launches, bugs, upstream/downstream effects.
5Recommend action — Propose fixes, further investigation, or monitoring plan.

Frameworks

Structuring cases, market sizing, and systematic problem solving

📋 Structuring a Case: 6-Step Framework

Follow this structured approach for any product/data case study:

1Clarify — Repeat the problem. Ask about scope, user segment, time frame, and success criteria.
2Define metrics — Choose a primary metric (north star) and supporting/guardrail metrics.
3Hypothesize — List possible causes or solutions. Prioritize by impact and feasibility.
4Analyze — Describe what data you'd pull, how you'd segment it, and what patterns to look for.
5Recommend — State your recommendation clearly. Back it with evidence from the analysis.
6Measure — Propose how to validate via A/B test or monitoring. Define success criteria.

🌍 Market Sizing

Two fundamental approaches to estimation questions:

⬇ Top-Down

Start from the total market and narrow down with filters:

  • Total population / users
  • Apply % filters (age, region, internet access)
  • Estimate adoption rate
  • Multiply by ARPU

Fast Big picture

⬆ Bottom-Up

Start from a single unit and scale up:

  • Revenue per transaction
  • Transactions per user/day
  • Number of users in a segment
  • Scale across segments

Precise Granular

Pro tip: Use both approaches as a sanity check. If they converge, your estimate is more credible.

Practice Cases

Experiment design principles and communication strategies

🔬 Experiment Design

Key principles for designing rigorous product experiments:

1Randomization — Randomly assign users to control/treatment to eliminate selection bias. Use hash-based assignment for consistency.
2Duration — Run long enough to capture weekly cycles (min 1-2 weeks). Account for novelty effects and learning curves.
3Guardrail metrics — Track metrics you do not want to harm (e.g., page load time, crash rate, customer support tickets) alongside your primary metric.
4Network effects — Watch for spillover between groups. If users interact (social, marketplace), consider cluster-based randomization or switchback designs.
Common pitfalls:
• Peeking at results too early → inflated false positives
• Multiple comparisons without correction
• Sample ratio mismatch (SRM) → check assignment
• Simpson's paradox in subgroup analysis

💬 Communication Tips

How to present your analysis in an interview or stakeholder meeting:

1Lead with the answer — State your recommendation first. Then walk through the supporting logic. Don't make them wait.
2Structure your response — Use numbered frameworks. Say "There are three factors..." so the listener can follow along.
3Quantify everything — "This would impact ~15% of users" is stronger than "some users." Back claims with numbers, even estimates.
4Acknowledge trade-offs — Show you understand the cost of your recommendation. "This improves X but may regress Y by ~2%."
