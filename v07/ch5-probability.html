Chapter V

Probability

Reasoning Under Uncertainty

5.1   Fundamentals

Probability is the mathematical language we use to quantify uncertainty. Before we can reason about machine learning models, A/B tests, or Bayesian inference, we need a firm grasp of the ground rules.

The Addition Rule

When we want the probability that at least one of two events occurs, we use the addition rule. For any two events A and B:

Addition Rule

P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A ∩ B)

We subtract the intersection because it gets counted twice — once in P(A) and once in P(B).

If A and B are mutually exclusive (they cannot both happen), the intersection is zero, and the formula simplifies to P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B).

The Multiplication Rule

When we want the probability that both events occur, we use the multiplication rule:

Multiplication Rule

P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B | A)

The probability of both A and B equals the probability of A times the probability of B given that A has occurred.

Independence

Two events are independent if learning that one occurred tells you absolutely nothing about whether the other occurred. Formally, A and B are independent when P(B | A) = P(B). Intuitively: learning A happened doesn't change P(B).

When events are independent, the multiplication rule simplifies beautifully to P(A ∩ B) = P(A) · P(B). This is what lets us, for instance, multiply the probability of getting heads on successive coin flips.

The Complement Technique

One of the most powerful problem-solving tricks in probability: sometimes it's far easier to count what doesn't happen than what does. Since P(A) = 1 − P(Ac), if computing P(A) directly is messy, try computing the complement instead.

Key Insight

"What is the probability that at least one person..." is almost always easier to solve as 1 − P(nobody does). This complementary counting technique appears in the birthday problem, network reliability, and dozens of interview questions.

Permutations vs. Combinations

Permutations count ordered arrangements. If you're choosing 3 flavors from 10 at an ice cream shop and the order matters (because the first scoop goes on the bottom), you have P(10, 3) = 10 × 9 × 8 = 720 possibilities.

Combinations count unordered selections. If you don't care which scoop goes where — you just want any 3 flavors — you divide out the 3! = 6 redundant orderings, giving C(10, 3) = 720 / 6 = 120 possibilities.

Counting Formulas

P(n, k) = n! / (n − k)!

C(n, k) = n! / (k! · (n − k)!)

Permutations when order matters. Combinations when it doesn't.

Example 5.1 — Committee Formation

How many ways can we form a committee of 3 from 10 people?

A committee is an unordered group — being "selected first" doesn't give you a special role. So we need combinations:

C(10, 3) = 10! / (3! · 7!) = (10 × 9 × 8) / (3 × 2 × 1) = 720 / 6 = 120 ways.

If the committee had a chair, secretary, and treasurer (distinct roles), we'd need permutations instead: P(10, 3) = 720.

5.2   Conditional Probability and Bayes' Theorem

Conditional Probability

Conditional probability answers the question: "Given that B has occurred, what is the probability of A?" It's about narrowing the sample space. Once we know B happened, B becomes our new universe, and we ask how much of B also contains A.

Conditional Probability

P(A | B) = P(A ∩ B) / P(B)

Read as: "the probability of A given B."

Deriving Bayes' Theorem

From the multiplication rule, we know that P(A ∩ B) = P(B | A) · P(A). We also know that P(A ∩ B) = P(A | B) · P(B). Setting these equal:

P(A | B) · P(B) = P(B | A) · P(A)

Dividing both sides by P(B) gives us Bayes' theorem:

Bayes' Theorem

P(A | B) = P(B | A) · P(A) / P(B)

Or in the language of Bayesian reasoning:

Posterior = (Likelihood × Prior) / Evidence

Prior — our belief before seeing data. Likelihood — how probable the data is under our hypothesis. Evidence — the overall probability of the data. Posterior — our updated belief.

Example 5.2 — The Medical Test Problem

Setup: A disease affects 1% of the population. A test for this disease is 99% accurate — it correctly identifies sick people 99% of the time (sensitivity), and correctly identifies healthy people 99% of the time (specificity). You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the disease?

Most people's intuition says "99%!" But the answer is roughly 50%. Let's work through it.

Define events:
D = has disease, Dc = healthy
+ = tests positive, − = tests negative

What we know:
P(D) = 0.01   (prevalence)
P(+ | D) = 0.99   (sensitivity)
P(− | Dc) = 0.99, so P(+ | Dc) = 0.01   (false positive rate)

Step 1: Find P(+) using the law of total probability:
P(+) = P(+ | D) · P(D) + P(+ | Dc) · P(Dc)
P(+) = 0.99 × 0.01 + 0.01 × 0.99
P(+) = 0.0099 + 0.0099 = 0.0198

Step 2: Apply Bayes' theorem:
P(D | +) = P(+ | D) · P(D) / P(+)
P(D | +) = 0.99 × 0.01 / 0.0198
P(D | +) = 0.0099 / 0.0198 = 0.50 (50%)

Why so low? The disease is rare. Out of 10,000 people, about 100 are sick and 9,900 are healthy. The test correctly flags 99 of the 100 sick people, but it also falsely flags 99 of the 9,900 healthy people. So among the ~198 positive results, only half actually have the disease. The base rate matters enormously.

5.3   Key Distributions

A probability distribution tells us how likely each outcome is. Mastering a handful of fundamental distributions equips you to model an enormous range of real-world phenomena.

Bernoulli & Binomial

A Bernoulli distribution models a single yes/no experiment: a coin flip, whether a customer clicks, whether a part is defective. It has one parameter p (the probability of success).

The Binomial distribution extends this to n independent trials. "Out of 100 manufactured parts, how many are defective?" If each part has a 2% defect rate independently, the number of defective parts follows Binomial(n=100, p=0.02).

Binomial Distribution

P(X = k) = C(n, k) · pk · (1 − p)n−k

Mean: E[X] = np   Variance: Var(X) = np(1 − p)

Poisson

The Poisson distribution models the number of events in a fixed interval when events occur independently at a constant average rate. How many customers arrive at a store per hour? How many typos on a page? How many server errors per day?

It has a single parameter λ (lambda), which is both the mean and the variance.

Poisson Distribution

P(X = k) = e−λ · λk / k!

Mean: E[X] = λ   Variance: Var(X) = λ

Geometric

The Geometric distribution models the number of trials until the first success. How many calls until a salesperson makes a sale? How many interviews until you get an offer? Each trial is independent with success probability p.

Geometric Distribution

P(X = k) = (1 − p)k−1 · p

Mean: E[X] = 1/p   Variance: Var(X) = (1 − p) / p2

Normal (Gaussian)

The Normal distribution is the most important distribution in statistics. It describes heights, measurement errors, test scores, and — thanks to the Central Limit Theorem — the distribution of sample means from virtually any population. Its familiar bell curve is defined by two parameters: the mean μ (center) and standard deviation σ (spread).

Normal Distribution

f(x) = (1 / σ√(2π)) · e−(x−μ)2 / (2σ2)

Mean: E[X] = μ   Variance: Var(X) = σ2

The 68-95-99.7 rule: ~68% of values fall within 1σ of the mean, ~95% within 2σ, ~99.7% within 3σ.

Exponential

The Exponential distribution models time between events in a Poisson process. If customers arrive at a rate of λ per hour (Poisson), then the time between arrivals follows an Exponential(λ) distribution. It is the only continuous distribution with the "memoryless" property: the probability of waiting another 5 minutes is the same whether you've already waited 2 minutes or 20.

Exponential Distribution

f(x) = λ · e−λx   for x ≥ 0

Mean: E[X] = 1/λ   Variance: Var(X) = 1/λ2

Summary of Distributions

Distribution Type Models Parameters Mean Variance
Bernoulli Discrete Single yes/no trial p p p(1-p)
Binomial Discrete Successes in n trials n, p np np(1-p)
Poisson Discrete Events in fixed interval λ λ λ
Geometric Discrete Trials until first success p 1/p (1-p)/p²
Normal Continuous Symmetric bell curve μ, σ μ σ²
Exponential Continuous Time between events λ 1/λ 1/λ²

5.4   Expected Value and Variance

Expected Value

The expected value of a random variable is its long-run average. If you repeated the experiment infinitely many times, E[X] is what the average outcome would converge to.

Expected Value

E[X] = Σ x · P(X = x)

Sum each possible value times its probability.

Variance

Variance measures the spread around the mean. A low variance means outcomes cluster tightly around E[X]; a high variance means they are widely dispersed.

Variance

Var(X) = E[(X − μ)2] = E[X2] − (E[X])2

Standard deviation σ = √Var(X) gives the spread in the same units as X.

Linearity of Expectation

One of the most elegant properties in probability: linearity of expectation always holds, even when variables are dependent.

Linearity of Expectation

E[aX + bY] = a · E[X] + b · E[Y]

This works for any random variables X and Y — they do not need to be independent! This seemingly simple property is incredibly powerful and is the key to solving many interview problems efficiently.

Example 5.3 — Expected Winnings in a Dice Game

You pay $5 to roll a fair die. If you roll a 6, you win $20. If you roll a 4 or 5, you win $10. Otherwise you win nothing. Should you play?

Calculate expected net winnings:

E[winnings] = P(6) · $20 + P(4 or 5) · $10 + P(1, 2, or 3) · $0 − $5
E[winnings] = (1/6) · 20 + (2/6) · 10 + (3/6) · 0 − 5
E[winnings] = 3.33 + 3.33 + 0 − 5
E[winnings] = $1.67

The expected value is positive — on average, you gain $1.67 per game. In the long run, this is a favorable bet.

5.5   The Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) formalizes something we all intuit: the more data you collect, the closer the sample average gets to the true expected value.

Flip a fair coin 10 times — you might easily get 7 heads (70%). That feels unremarkable. Flip it 10,000 times and getting 7,000 heads would be astonishing. The LLN guarantees that as your sample size grows, the sample mean converges to the population mean.

Law of Large Numbers

As n → ∞, the sample mean X̄n converges in probability to E[X] = μ.

In plain terms: more data means more reliable estimates.

Why This Matters for Data Science

The LLN is the theoretical backbone of A/B testing. When you run an experiment, the conversion rate you observe is a sample mean. With a small sample, it's noisy — you might see a "winner" that's really just random fluctuation. With a large enough sample, the observed rate will be close to the true rate.

This is why sample size matters. It's why we run experiments for weeks rather than hours. And it's why "we tried it once and it didn't work" is not a valid statistical argument.

Key Insight

The LLN tells us sample means converge to the true mean. The Central Limit Theorem (Chapter IV) tells us how they converge — normally, with standard error σ/√n. Together, they are the foundation of statistical inference.

5.6   Classic Interview Puzzles

These problems show up in interviews not because the answers are useful in daily work, but because they test your ability to reason carefully under uncertainty. Let's break each one down.

The Birthday Problem

Question: How many people do you need in a room before there's a ≥50% chance that two share a birthday?

Approach: Use complementary counting. Instead of computing the probability that some pair shares a birthday (messy), compute the probability that everyone has a unique birthday, then subtract from 1.

With n people in a room (and 365 possible birthdays):

Birthday Problem — Complementary Counting

P(all unique) = (365/365) · (364/365) · (363/365) · ... · ((365−n+1)/365)

P(at least one match) = 1 − P(all unique)

The first person can have any birthday. The second must avoid 1 date (364/365). The third must avoid 2 dates (363/365). And so on.

Working through the numbers: at n = 23, P(at least one match) crosses 50%. At n = 50, it exceeds 97%. The answer is surprisingly small because the number of pairs grows much faster than the number of people: 23 people form C(23, 2) = 253 pairs, each a potential match.

The Monty Hall Problem

Setup: You're on a game show with 3 doors. One hides a car; the other two hide goats. You pick a door. The host, who knows what's behind each door, opens a different door to reveal a goat. Should you switch?

Answer: Yes. Switching wins 2/3 of the time. Here's why:

Consider all possible scenarios when you initially pick Door 1:

  • Car is behind Door 1 (prob 1/3): Host opens Door 2 or 3 (goat). Switching → you lose.
  • Car is behind Door 2 (prob 1/3): Host must open Door 3 (the only goat). Switching to Door 2 → you win.
  • Car is behind Door 3 (prob 1/3): Host must open Door 2 (the only goat). Switching to Door 3 → you win.

Switching wins in 2 out of 3 equally likely scenarios. The key insight is that your initial pick has a 1/3 chance of being correct and the host's action doesn't change that — it just concentrates the remaining 2/3 probability onto the one unopened door.

The Coupon Collector Problem

Question: A cereal box contains one of n different coupons (each equally likely). How many boxes do you need to buy, on average, to collect all n coupons?

After collecting k unique coupons, the probability that the next box gives you a new one is (n − k)/n. The expected number of boxes to get the next new coupon is therefore n/(n − k), which is a geometric random variable.

Using linearity of expectation, the total expected boxes needed is:

Coupon Collector

E[T] = n/n + n/(n−1) + n/(n−2) + ... + n/1 = n · H(n)

Where H(n) = 1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + ... + 1/n is the n-th harmonic number.

For large n: H(n) ≈ ln(n) + 0.5772, so E[T] ≈ n · ln(n).

For example, with n = 50 coupons: E[T] ≈ 50 × (ln(50) + 0.5772) ≈ 50 × 4.49 ≈ 225 boxes. The last few coupons take disproportionately long to find — a pattern familiar to anyone who has tried to complete a collection.

Key Insight

These classic puzzles test your ability to reason carefully, not memorize formulas. Interviewers are looking for clear problem decomposition, correct use of complementary counting and conditional probability, and the ability to check whether an answer passes the "smell test." When in doubt, try a small example first.
