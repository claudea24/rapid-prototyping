Probability — Reasoning Under Uncertainty

Probability is the mathematical language for quantifying uncertainty. Mastering it means you can reason precisely about randomness, risk, and evidence — skills tested in every data science interview.

5.1

Fundamentals

▼

Probability assigns a number between 0 and 1 to events. These core rules let you combine, decompose, and count probabilities for any situation you encounter. Every complex problem reduces to applying these building blocks.

Addition Rule: When you want the probability that either event A or event B occurs, you add their individual probabilities and subtract the overlap — because the intersection gets counted in both P(A) and P(B).

Multiplication Rule: When you want the probability that both A and B occur, multiply by the conditional. This chains naturally: P(A∩B∩C) = P(A)·P(B|A)·P(C|A∩B).

Independence: Two events are independent if learning one occurred tells you nothing about the other. The multiplication rule simplifies because P(B|A) = P(B).

Complement: Sometimes it is far easier to count what doesn't happen. P(at least one) = 1 − P(none).

Counting: Permutations count ordered arrangements; combinations count unordered selections. Knowing which to use is half the battle in discrete probability.

"At least one..." problems → solve as 1 − P(nobody does). Complementary counting appears everywhere in probability interviews and simplifies seemingly complex problems into one clean calculation.

5.2

Bayes' Theorem

▼ 
P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B)

Read it as: Prior × Likelihood / Evidence = Posterior. Bayes' theorem tells you how to update your beliefs when you observe new evidence. The prior is what you believed before; the likelihood is how probable the evidence is under your hypothesis; the evidence is the total probability of seeing what you saw; the posterior is your updated belief.

Worked Example — Medical Test

A disease affects 1% of the population. A test is 99% accurate (detects the disease when present, and correctly clears healthy people). You test positive. What is the probability you actually have the disease?

  • P(D) = 0.01 — prior probability of disease
  • P(+|D) = 0.99 — true positive rate (sensitivity)
  • P(+|D') = 0.01 — false positive rate

Step 1: Total probability of testing positive:

P(+) = P(+|D)·P(D) + P(+|D')·P(D') = 0.99×0.01 + 0.01×0.99 = 0.0198

Step 2: Apply Bayes:

P(D|+) = 0.0099 / 0.0198 = 50%

Why only 50%? Think in natural frequencies. Out of 10,000 people: 100 have the disease, 99 of them test positive. Of the 9,900 healthy people, 99 also test positive (false positives). So 99 true positives vs 99 false positives — exactly half. The low base rate (1%) dramatically dilutes the test's accuracy.

5.4

Expected Value & Variance

▼

Expected value is the long-run average outcome. Variance measures how spread out the outcomes are around that average. Together they summarise a distribution's centre and width.

E[X] = Σ x · P(x)

Var(X) = E[X²] − (E[X])²

Linearity of expectation: E[aX + bY] = a·E[X] + b·E[Y]. This works even when X and Y are dependent, which makes it incredibly powerful. You can break a complex random variable into simple indicator variables and sum their expectations without worrying about correlations.

Worked Example — Dice Game

You pay $5 to roll a fair die. Roll a 6 → win $20. Roll a 4 or 5 → win $10. Otherwise → win $0.

Expected net gain:

E = (1/6)·$20 + (2/6)·$10 + (3/6)·$0 − $5

E = $3.33 + $3.33 + $0 − $5 = $1.67

Positive expected value — you should play this game. Over many rounds, you gain about $1.67 per play on average.

Key Insight

Linearity of expectation is arguably the most useful tool in discrete probability. The expected number of fixed points in a random permutation? Just sum n indicators each with E = 1/n, giving E = 1. No inclusion-exclusion needed.

5.5

Law of Large Numbers

▼

The Law of Large Numbers says that as you collect more observations, the sample average converges to the true expected value. This is why A/B tests work: with enough users, the observed conversion rates stabilise around the true rates, and you can detect real differences from noise.

It does not mean that short-term deviations are "corrected" — the gambler's fallacy. A coin that flipped 10 heads in a row is not "due" for tails. Instead, future flips dilute the early streak, making the overall proportion drift toward 0.5.

5.6

Classic Interview Puzzles

▼

The Birthday Problem: How many people do you need in a room before there's a >50% chance two share a birthday? The answer is just 23. It feels impossibly low because we focus on one person, but there are C(23,2) = 253 pairs, each with a 1/365 chance of matching. Using complementary counting: P(no match among n) = (365/365)·(364/365)·...·((365−n+1)/365). At n = 23, this drops below 0.5.

The Monty Hall Problem: You pick door 1 of 3. The host (who knows what's behind each door) opens door 3 to reveal a goat. Should you switch to door 2? Yes — switching wins 2/3 of the time. Your initial pick had a 1/3 chance. The remaining 2/3 probability collapsed onto the one unopened door. The host's action doesn't change your original odds; it just concentrates the "other" probability.

Coupon Collector: If there are n distinct coupons and you collect one uniformly at random each day, the expected number of days to collect all n is E[T] = n · H(n) where H(n) is the n-th harmonic number. For n = 50: E[T] ≈ 50 × 4.499 ≈ 225 days. Each new coupon gets harder — the last one takes an expected n days alone.
