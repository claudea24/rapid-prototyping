Machine Learning — From Theory to Practice

Machine learning is pattern recognition at scale. This chapter builds your mental model from algorithms and evaluation metrics to the bias-variance tradeoff and feature engineering — the concepts that separate practitioners from beginners.

3.1

Supervised Algorithms

▼

Supervised learning is the workhorse of applied ML: you have labeled data and you want to predict the label for new, unseen observations. The art is choosing the right algorithm for your data's characteristics — dimensionality, linearity, size, and interpretability requirements.

Logistic RegressionProbabilistic, regularizableLinear boundary onlyBinary classification

3.2

Unsupervised Algorithms

▼

Unsupervised learning finds structure in data without labels. The two main flavors are clustering (grouping similar observations) and dimensionality reduction (compressing features while preserving structure). The right choice depends on whether you know how many groups to expect, the shape of your clusters, and whether you need interpretability or just visualization.

AlgorithmTypeKey PropertyUse When

3.3

Evaluation Metrics

▼

A model is only as good as the metric you evaluate it with. Choosing the wrong metric can lead you to optimize for the wrong thing — high accuracy on an imbalanced dataset is meaningless if your model never predicts the minority class.

MetricFormula IdeaFails WhenUse When
Accuracy(TP+TN) / totalImbalanced classesBalanced datasets
PrecisionTP / (TP+FP)—Cost of FP is high (spam filter)
RecallTP / (TP+FN)—Cost of FN is high (disease detection)
F1Harmonic mean P&R—Need balance of P and R
AUC-ROCArea under TPR vs FPR—Comparing models, threshold-independent
RMSE√(mean squared errors)—Penalize large errors (regression)
MAEMean absolute errors—Treat all errors equally (regression)
Key Insight

Before choosing metrics, ask: what is the cost of a false positive vs. a false negative? The answer drives everything. A cancer screening model that misses tumors (low recall) is far worse than one that flags a few healthy patients (low precision).

3.4

Bias-Variance Tradeoff

▼

The dartboard analogy: Imagine throwing darts at a target. High bias means your darts consistently land in the wrong area — your aim is off (systematic error). High variance means your darts are scattered all over — inconsistent (sensitivity to training data). The goal is low bias AND low variance: darts clustered tightly on the bullseye.

Total error = Bias² + Variance + Irreducible noise. As you increase model complexity, bias decreases (model can capture more patterns) but variance increases (model starts fitting noise). The sweet spot is where total error is minimized.

ScenarioBiasVarianceSymptomFix
UnderfittingHighLowPoor on train AND testMore features, complex model
Sweet spotLowLowGood on bothKeep it
OverfittingLowHighGreat train, poor testRegularization, more data, simpler

3.5

Feature Engineering & Cross-Validation

▼

Feature engineering is the art of transforming raw data into features that better represent the underlying problem to the model. It's where domain knowledge meets data science — and often matters more than the model you choose.

TechniqueWhenWatch Out
One-hot encodingNominal categoriesHigh cardinality → many columns
Label encodingOrdinal categoriesDon't use for nominal
StandardScalerKNN, SVM, logistic, PCANot needed for trees
Log transformRight-skewed dataRemember to reverse: exp(y)-1
Target encodingHigh-cardinalityData leakage risk

Cross-validation is your defense against overfitting during model selection. Instead of a single train/test split (which is noisy), k-fold CV splits the data into k folds, trains on k-1 folds, and validates on the held-out fold — rotating k times. The average score is a more reliable estimate of generalization performance.

Common choices: 5-fold or 10-fold for general use. Stratified k-fold for imbalanced classification (preserves class ratios). Time-series split for temporal data (never train on the future). Leave-one-out for very small datasets (high variance, expensive).

Key Insight

Feature engineering often matters more than model choice. Simple logistic regression on well-crafted features beats gradient boosting on raw features. Spend 80% of your time on data and features, 20% on models.
