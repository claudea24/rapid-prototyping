Statistics — The Foundation of Inference

Statistics gives us the tools to draw conclusions from data, quantify uncertainty, and make decisions under incomplete information. This chapter builds from descriptive measures through hypothesis testing to experimental design.

4.1

Descriptive Statistics

▼

Before modeling, you must understand your data. Descriptive statistics summarize the center, spread, and shape of a distribution. But choosing the wrong summary statistic is a common and quiet mistake — reporting the mean of a heavily skewed distribution is misleading.

Key Insight

Always ask: is my data skewed? If yes, prefer median and IQR over mean and standard deviation. Wrong summary statistic is common, quiet damage — your stakeholders will draw wrong conclusions from a misleading average.

4.2

Hypothesis Testing

▼

Hypothesis testing is a structured framework for making decisions from data. It does not tell you what is true — it tells you whether the data is surprising enough to reject a specific assumption.

  1. State the null hypothesis (H0): The default assumption (e.g., "no difference between groups")
  2. State the alternative (H1): What you're trying to show (e.g., "treatment group has higher mean")
  3. Choose significance level α: Usually 0.05 — your tolerance for false positives
  4. Select the test: Based on data type, sample size, and assumptions (see 4.3)
  5. Compute the test statistic and p-value: How extreme is the observed data under H0?
  6. Decision: If p < α, reject H0. Otherwise, fail to reject (do NOT say "accept H0")

What a p-value actually means: The probability of observing data at least as extreme as yours, assuming H0 is true. A p-value of 0.03 means "if there really is no effect, there's a 3% chance of seeing data this extreme."

What it does NOT mean: (1) It is NOT the probability that H0 is true. (2) It is NOT the probability of replicating the result. These are common and dangerous misinterpretations.

4.3

Common Statistical Tests

▼

Choosing the right test — decision flowchart:

  1. Is your outcome variable continuous or categorical?
  2. If continuous: how many groups are you comparing? (1, 2, or 3+)
  3. If 2 groups: are they independent or paired?
  4. Can you assume normality? (Check with Shapiro-Wilk or Q-Q plot)
  5. If normal → parametric test (t-test, ANOVA). If not → non-parametric (Mann-Whitney, Kruskal-Wallis)
  6. If categorical outcome: use Chi-squared or Fisher's exact test

4.4

Confidence Intervals

▼

Formula: CI = x̄ ± z* · (σ / √n)

Correct interpretation: If we repeated this experiment many times, 95% of the computed intervals would contain the true population parameter. It does NOT mean "there is a 95% probability the true value is in this interval" — the true value is fixed, not random.

4.5

A/B Testing

▼

The 6-step A/B testing process:

  1. Define the hypothesis: "Changing X will increase metric Y by at least Z%"
  2. Choose primary metric: One metric to make the go/no-go decision
  3. Calculate sample size: Based on baseline rate, MDE, alpha, and power
  4. Randomize and run: Random assignment, no peeking at results
  5. Analyze: Compare groups with the pre-chosen test at the pre-chosen alpha
  6. Decide and document: Ship, iterate, or discard — and record learnings

4.6

Central Limit Theorem

▼

Statement: Regardless of the population distribution, the distribution of sample means approaches a normal distribution as sample size increases. The mean of the sampling distribution equals the population mean, and its standard deviation (the standard error) equals σ / √n.

Intuition: Each sample mean is the average of many random values. Extreme values in either direction tend to cancel out, pulling each mean toward the center. The more values you average (larger n), the stronger this centering effect becomes — which is why the bell shape tightens with larger samples.

Why it matters: The CLT is what makes confidence intervals and hypothesis tests work. Even if your raw data is wildly non-normal (exponential, uniform, bimodal), the sample mean is approximately normal for large enough n. This lets us use z-scores and t-distributions for inference.

Key Insight

CLT tells us HOW sample means converge — normally, with SE = σ/√n. Together with the Law of Large Numbers (which says they converge at all), this forms the foundation of statistical inference.
