The ML Story — 2 Scenes

Algorithms, evaluation, bias-variance, and feature engineering through company narratives.

Scene 1 Algorithms + Evaluation

▾
The Engagement Model for Instagram stories is underperforming. The team tried logistic regression but it's too simple — the relationship is clearly nonlinear. Your interviewer asks: "Walk me through the algorithm options and how you'd evaluate them."
The Spam Classifier for Gmail is flagging legitimate emails. The current model optimizes for accuracy, but with 99.5% non-spam, that metric is meaningless. Your interviewer asks: "What algorithm would you choose and which metric actually matters?"
The Recommendation Engine's click-through rate has plateaued. The current random forest was never tuned. Your interviewer asks: "How would you compare algorithms and what metrics would you track?"

Evaluation Metrics

MetricFormula IdeaFails WhenUse When
Accuracy(TP+TN) / totalImbalanced classesBalanced datasets
PrecisionTP / (TP+FP)—Cost of FP is high (spam filter)
RecallTP / (TP+FN)—Cost of FN is high (disease detection)
F1 ScoreHarmonic mean of P & R—Balance precision and recall

Key Insight

Accuracy is dangerously misleading with imbalanced data. A fraud detector predicting "not fraud" for everything achieves 99.5% accuracy but catches zero fraud. Always ask: what is the cost of false positives vs. false negatives?

Practice Exercise

Your model has precision = 0.95 and recall = 0.60. Compute the F1 score. Then explain: would you prioritize improving precision or recall for a phishing email detector?

Scene 2 Bias-Variance + Feature Engineering

▾
Your engagement model's training accuracy is 98% but test accuracy is 72%. Classic overfitting. The interviewer asks: "Explain the bias-variance tradeoff and how you'd fix this."
The spam classifier performs perfectly on training data but poorly on new emails. Your Google interviewer asks: "What's happening here? Walk me through bias, variance, and your feature engineering approach."
The recommendation engine's accuracy varies wildly across product categories. Some categories overfit, others underfit. "How do you diagnose and fix this?" the Amazon interviewer asks.
Scene Timer 00:00

Bias is the error from overly simplistic assumptions — the model can't capture the true pattern (underfitting). Variance is the error from sensitivity to training data fluctuations — the model memorizes noise (overfitting). The goal is finding the sweet spot.

ProblemSymptomFix
High Bias (underfitting)Both train and test error are highMore complex model, more features, less regularization
High Variance (overfitting)Low train error, high test errorMore data, regularization (L1/L2), simpler model, dropout

Feature Engineering

TechniqueWhat It DoesWhen to Use
One-Hot EncodingConverts categories to binary columnsNominal categories, low cardinality
Standard ScalingZero mean, unit varianceDistance-based models (KNN, SVM)
Log TransformCompresses right-skewed distributionsRevenue, prices, counts
Target EncodingReplaces category with mean of targetHigh-cardinality categories
Interaction FeaturesMultiplies/combines featuresWhen relationships are multiplicative
Key Insight

Regularization adds a penalty for complexity: L1 (Lasso) drives some coefficients to exactly zero (feature selection), while L2 (Ridge) shrinks all coefficients toward zero (smooth, stable). Use L1 when you suspect many features are irrelevant; L2 when all features matter but need dampening.

Practice Exercise

You have a feature "city" with 500 unique values. Explain why one-hot encoding would be problematic and propose two alternatives.
