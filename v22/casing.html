The Case Studies Story — 2 Scenes

AARRR metrics, metric diagnosis, market sizing, and communication through company narratives.

Scene 1 AARRR + Metric Diagnosis

▾
The Product Review at Meta: "Facebook Groups DAU dropped 15% last week. Walk me through how you'd investigate." Your interviewer wants you to use the AARRR framework to structure your analysis before touching any data.
The Growth Case at Google: "YouTube Shorts engagement is flat despite a 20% increase in content creators. Diagnose the problem." You need to decompose the metric using the pirate framework and segment methodically.
The Metrics Review at Amazon: "Prime Video watch time dropped 8% month-over-month. The content library grew. What's going on?" Your interviewer expects structured thinking, not guessing.

Diagnosing Metric Changes: The 6-Step Framework

1. Clarify — Restate the problem. Which product? Which platform? What time window?
2. Define Metrics — Primary metric + guardrail metrics.
3. Hypothesize — State hypotheses before touching data.
4. Analyze — Segment by platform, cohort, geography, time.
5. Recommend — Concrete action + expected impact estimate.
6. Measure — How will you validate? What experiment?

Key Insight

Always check "is it a data problem?" first. Did a schema migration break tracking? Is there a processing delay? Data problems are embarrassingly common — rule them out before investigating product changes.

Practice Exercise

"Your app's DAU dropped 10% last Tuesday." Walk through the 6-step framework. List 3 specific hypotheses and describe how you'd validate each one.

Scene 2 Market Sizing + Communication

▾
Your Meta interviewer asks: "How many Instagram Reels are uploaded per day in the US?" Then: "Now present your findings to a VP who has 2 minutes. What do you say first?" This tests both estimation and communication.
Your Google growth case: "How many piano tuners are there in Chicago?" The classic Fermi estimation. Then: "The VP of Search wants to know if we should launch a piano-tuning marketplace. Make your case in 60 seconds."
Your Amazon metrics review: "How many packages does Amazon deliver per day in the US?" Then: "The SVP asks why last-mile delivery costs went up 5%. Structure your answer using the pyramid principle."

Market Sizing: Two Approaches

ApproachMethodBest For
Top-DownStart from total market, narrow by percentagesBroad markets with known population data
Bottom-UpStart from a unit and multiply upSpecific products, local markets

Example (bottom-up): Piano tuners in Chicago. ~2.5M households. ~5% have pianos = 125K pianos. Each tuned ~once/year = 125K tunings/year. Each tuning takes ~2 hours + travel = 4 tunings/day per tuner. 250 working days = 1,000 tunings/tuner/year. Answer: 125K / 1,000 = ~125 piano tuners.

Communicating Your Analysis

The Pyramid Principle: Lead with the answer, then provide supporting evidence. Executives want the conclusion first, details second. Structure: (1) Bottom line, (2) 2–3 supporting points, (3) Data if asked.

PrincipleWhat It MeansExample
Lead with the answerState your conclusion first"The drop is caused by an iOS crash bug."
Quantify everythingAttach numbers to claims"Affecting 22% of iOS users since Tuesday."
Present trade-offsShow you considered alternatives"Hotfix costs 2 eng-days; waiting costs $50K/day."
Close with next stepsEnd with a clear recommendation"I recommend the hotfix. I'll track recovery daily."
Key Insight

In market sizing, being within 2–5x of the actual answer is "correct." The interviewer cares about your approach: did you structure it clearly? Did you state and sanity-check your assumptions? Did you arrive at a reasonable number?

Practice Exercise

Estimate the number of Uber rides per day in New York City using a bottom-up approach. State your assumptions clearly and sanity-check the final number.
