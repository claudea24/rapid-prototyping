The Statistics Story — 2 Scenes

Hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, A/B testing, and CLT through company narratives.

Scene 1 Hypothesis Testing + Confidence Intervals

▾
The A/B Experiment for a new Facebook onboarding flow is showing a 1.8% lift in conversion. Your PM asks: "Is this real or just noise?" You need to formalize the question as a hypothesis test.
The Experiment Platform team asks you to validate a new search ranking algorithm. Initial results show a 0.5% improvement in click-through rate. "Run the stats," the tech lead says. "We don't ship unless p < 0.05."
The Prime Experiment team launched a new checkout flow for Prime members. Conversion moved from 68% to 69.2%. "Is that statistically significant with our sample size?" the VP of Product asks.
Scene Timer 00:00

The hypothesis testing framework has six steps: (1) Formulate H0 and H1, (2) Choose significance level alpha (usually 0.05), (3) Collect data, (4) Compute test statistic, (5) Compare to critical value or compute p-value, (6) Conclude: reject or fail to reject H0.

ErrorNameAnalogyProbability
Type IFalse PositiveConvicting an innocent personα (usually 0.05)
Type IIFalse NegativeAcquitting a guilty personβ
PowerCorrect RejectionCatching the guilty1 − β (target ≥ 0.80)
Key Insight

The p-value is the probability of observing data at least as extreme as yours, assuming H0 is true. It does NOT mean the probability that H0 is true. It does NOT mean the probability the result is due to chance.

Confidence Intervals

A 95% confidence interval means: if you repeated the experiment many times, 95% of the computed intervals would contain the true parameter. It does NOT mean there's a 95% probability the true value is in this particular interval.

Width of a CI depends on: confidence level (higher = wider), sample size (larger = narrower), and variability (more variance = wider). Doubling precision requires quadrupling sample size.

Practice Exercise

A new feature has baseline conversion 12%. You observe 13.8% in a sample of 2,000 users. Calculate the z-statistic and determine if the result is significant at alpha = 0.05.

Scene 2 A/B Testing + CLT

▾
Your Facebook experiment shows significance on Day 3, but the experimentation team says "Don't peek!" Why does checking results early inflate your false positive rate? You need to understand the peeking problem and proper A/B methodology.
Google's experiment platform runs thousands of A/B tests simultaneously. "How do we handle the multiple comparisons problem?" your interviewer asks. "If we test 100 hypotheses, we expect 5 false positives at alpha = 0.05."
The Prime experiment results look great, but the VP asks: "How do we know this isn't just a novelty effect? And what about network effects — Prime users interact with non-Prime users." Time to think about A/B testing pitfalls.

Central Limit Theorem

The CLT states that the sampling distribution of the sample mean approaches a normal distribution as sample size increases, regardless of the population's shape. This is why we can use z-tests and t-tests even for non-normal data.

Standard Error = σ / √n. As n increases, the sampling distribution gets narrower. This is why larger samples give more precise estimates and narrower confidence intervals.

Key Insight

The CLT is the foundation that makes most statistical inference work in practice. Even if individual user session times are heavily right-skewed, the average session time across 10,000 users will be approximately normally distributed.

Practice Exercise
