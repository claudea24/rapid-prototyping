The Probability Story — 2 Scenes

Bayes' theorem, distributions, expected value, and classic puzzles through company narratives.

Scene 1 Bayes' Theorem + Distributions

▾
The Ad Auction system flags suspicious ad accounts using an automated test. The test is 99% accurate, but only 1% of accounts are actually fraudulent. A flagged account appeals. What's the probability they're actually fraudulent? This is Bayes' theorem in action.
The Interview Puzzle round at Google: "A rare disease affects 1 in 1,000 people. A test is 95% accurate. You test positive. What's the probability you have the disease?" Your interviewer watches you work through Bayes' theorem.
The Demand Forecast team uses a Bayesian model to predict holiday sales. "Walk me through how you'd update your prior belief about demand given new sales data," your interviewer says. This is applied Bayes.
Scene Timer 00:00

Bayes' Theorem: P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B). In words: Posterior = (Likelihood × Prior) / Evidence.

Consider the classic medical test: Disease prevalence P(D) = 0.01. Test sensitivity P(+|D) = 0.99. False positive rate P(+|D') = 0.01.

P(+) = P(+|D) · P(D) + P(+|D') · P(D')
     = 0.99 × 0.01 + 0.01 × 0.99
     = 0.0099 + 0.0099 = 0.0198

P(D|+) = P(+|D) · P(D) / P(+)
       = 0.0099 / 0.0198 = 0.50 (50%!)
Key Insight

Even with a 99% accurate test, a positive result only means 50% probability of disease when prevalence is 1%. The base rate dominates. This is the "base rate fallacy" — one of the most commonly tested probability concepts.

Scene 2 Expected Value + Puzzles

▾
Your interviewer at Meta asks: "In our ad auction, each advertiser bids for impressions. If an ad has a 3% click probability and generates $2.50 per click, what's the expected revenue per impression? How does this change with 1,000 impressions?"
Your Google interview puzzle: "You roll a fair die. I pay you the face value in dollars. What's the fair price to play this game?" Then: "What if you can re-roll once?" This is expected value with optimal stopping.
The Demand Forecast interview: "If daily demand follows a Poisson distribution with λ=50, and each unfulfilled order costs $10 while each excess unit costs $2, how many units should we stock?" This is expected value optimization.
Scene Timer 00:00

Expected Value: E[X] = Σ x·P(x). It is the long-run average. Linearity of Expectation: E[X+Y] = E[X] + E[Y], always — even if X and Y are dependent. This powerful property simplifies many interview problems.

Variance: Var(X) = E[X²] − (E[X])². For independent X and Y: Var(X+Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y).

Key Insight

Linearity of expectation is the most useful tool in probability interviews. "Expected number of coin flips to get all heads at least once with n coins" = sum of expected values for each coin, regardless of dependence.
