The Casino Problem — Probability

A gaming company wants to launch a new feature. Your job: calculate the odds and make sure the math works.

A mobile gaming company is launching a new "Lucky Spin" feature — users spend coins for a chance to win prizes. The game designer sketched out reward tiers, but nobody checked the math. The CFO asks: "What's the expected payout per spin? Will we lose money?" You need to figure it out.

Scene 1 The Fundamentals — Rules & Bayes

▾
Before calculating the payout, you need to review the probability rules. The designer combined independent and dependent events without distinguishing them. And the fraud team wants to know: if a user wins 5 times in a row, what's the probability they're cheating? That's Bayes' theorem.
RuleFormulaKey Point
AdditionP(A∪B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A∩B)Subtract intersection (counted twice)
MultiplicationP(A∩B) = P(A) · P(B|A)Chain conditional probabilities
IndependenceP(A∩B) = P(A) · P(B)Learning A doesn't change P(B)

Bayes' Theorem: P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B). In words: Posterior = (Likelihood × Prior) / Evidence.

The Medical Test Problem: A disease affects 1% of the population. A test is 99% accurate. You test positive. What's the probability you have the disease? Intuition says 99%. Reality: ~50%.

# P(D) = 0.01 (prevalence)
# P(+|D) = 0.99 (sensitivity)
# P(+|D') = 0.01 (false positive rate)

P_pos = 0.99 * 0.01 + 0.01 * 0.99  # = 0.0198
P_D_given_pos = (0.99 * 0.01) / P_pos  # = 0.50 (50%!)

# Why? 10,000 people: 100 sick (99 test +), 9,900 healthy (99 test +)
# Total positives: 198. Only 99 are actually diseased.
# The BASE RATE (1%) dominates the calculation.
Key Insight

"At least one..." problems → solve as 1 − P(nobody does). This complementary counting appears in the birthday problem, network reliability, and dozens of interview questions.

Now you understand the rules. But the Lucky Spin has different prize tiers with different probabilities. You need to match each tier to the right distribution.

Scene 2 The Prize Tiers — Key Distributions

▾
The Lucky Spin has binary outcomes (win/lose each tier), a fixed number of spins per day, and a "time until jackpot" mechanic. Each maps to a different probability distribution. You need to pick the right one for each calculation.

For the Lucky Spin: Each spin is Bernoulli (win/lose). 10 spins per day → Binomial for "how many wins today." The jackpot trigger (average 1 per 500 spins) → Poisson. "How many spins until first jackpot" → Geometric. The 68-95-99.7 rule: ~68% of data falls within 1σ, ~95% within 2σ, ~99.7% within 3σ.

Key Insight

The Poisson approximates the Binomial when n is large and p is small (λ = np). The Exponential is the continuous analog of the Geometric — both have the memoryless property: the time you've already waited doesn't affect the probability of waiting longer.

Distributions mapped. Now for the question the CFO actually cares about: what's the expected value of each spin? And two classic puzzles the game designer accidentally recreated.

Scene 3 The Expected Value — Classic Puzzles

▾
The CFO wants one number: expected payout per spin. And the game designer's "Mystery Door" bonus round is literally the Monty Hall problem. The "Collector's Album" feature? That's the coupon collector problem. You've seen these before.

Expected Value: E[X] = Σ x · P(X = x). The long-run average. If you played a game infinitely many times, the average payoff would converge to E[X]. Linearity of expectation works even when variables are dependent: E[aX + bY] = a·E[X] + b·E[Y].

# Lucky Spin expected payout:
# Cost per spin: 100 coins
# Jackpot (1/500): 10,000 coins
# Big win (1/50): 500 coins
# Small win (1/5): 50 coins
# Nothing (else): 0 coins

E_payout = (1/500) * 10000 + (1/50) * 500 + (1/5) * 50
         = 20 + 10 + 10
         = 40 coins per spin

# Cost: 100 coins. Payout: 40 coins.
# House edge: 60 coins per spin (60%). Feature is profitable.

The Monty Hall Problem (Mystery Door bonus): 3 doors, 1 prize, 2 blanks. You pick a door. Host opens a blank. Should you switch? Yes — switching wins 2/3 of the time. Your initial pick has 1/3 chance. The other two doors have 2/3 collectively. When the host reveals a blank, all 2/3 concentrates on the remaining door.

The Birthday Problem (Collector's Album): How many people until a ≥50% chance two share a birthday? Just 23. Because 23 people = C(23,2) = 253 pairs, each with 1/365 chance of matching. With 253 "chances," a match becomes likely.

The Coupon Collector (Album completion): On average, how many items to collect all n unique items? E[T] = n · H(n) ≈ n · (ln(n) + 0.58). For n=50 items: ~225 draws.

Key Insight

These puzzles test your ability to decompose problems, apply conditional probability correctly, and verify your answer makes sense. When unsure, try a small example first (2 doors, 3 people) to build intuition before scaling up.

Story Resolution

Expected value per spin: +60 coins for the house. The feature is profitable with a 60% margin. You also flag that the Mystery Door bonus should let users switch (it's more fun and mathematically interesting), and the Collector's Album needs ~225 draws on average for 50 items — which maps to about 3 weeks of daily play. The CFO approves, the designer tweaks the numbers, and the feature launches to 4.2 stars on the app store.
