The Prediction Game — Machine Learning

Marketing wants to predict churn. You need to build a model that actually works in production, not just on a Jupyter notebook.

The Head of Marketing pulls you into a meeting: "We're spending $2M/month on re-engagement campaigns, but we're spraying and praying. Can you build a model that predicts which users will churn next month? If we target the right 20%, we save half the budget."

Scene 1 Choose Your Model — Supervised Learning

▾
You have labeled data — users who churned last quarter and users who didn't. This is a binary classification problem. But which algorithm? Logistic regression for interpretability? Random Forest for robustness? Gradient boosting for accuracy? Each has trade-offs.

Logistic Regression models log-odds as a linear function. Coefficients are directly interpretable. Random Forest trains many trees on random subsets and averages — low variance, robust. Gradient Boosting builds trees sequentially, each correcting the prior's errors — state-of-the-art for tabular data.

Key Insight

For churn prediction, you likely want both: a logistic regression baseline for interpretability (stakeholders want to know why users churn) and a gradient boosting model for production accuracy. Start simple, add complexity only if needed.

You start with logistic regression as a baseline and Random Forest as the candidate. Both train quickly. But how do you know which is better? You need the right evaluation metric — and for churn, accuracy is the wrong one.

Scene 2 Evaluate It — Metrics That Matter

▾
Your first model reports 94% accuracy. Impressive? No — only 6% of users actually churn. A model that predicts "no churn" for everyone gets 94% accuracy while catching zero churners. You need precision and recall.

Precision answers: "Of all users I flagged as churners, how many actually churned?" Recall answers: "Of all actual churners, how many did I catch?" For churn, recall matters more — missing a churner means losing them forever.

MetricIdeaFails WhenUse When
Accuracy(TP+TN) / totalImbalanced classesBalanced datasets
PrecisionTP / (TP+FP)—Cost of FP is high (spam filter)
RecallTP / (TP+FN)—Cost of FN is high (churn, disease)
F1 ScoreHarmonic mean of P & R—Need balance of both
AUC-ROCArea under TPR vs FPR—Comparing models, threshold-free
Key Insight

Before choosing a metric, always ask: "What is the cost of a false positive versus a false negative?" For churn, a false negative means losing a customer ($50 LTV). A false positive means sending a discount to someone who would have stayed ($5 wasted). Recall should dominate.

With recall as your north star, the Random Forest outperforms logistic regression: 78% recall vs 62%. But when you test on a holdout set, recall drops to 51%. Something is wrong — your model is overfitting.

Scene 3 The Bias-Variance Trap — Overfitting

▾
Your senior data scientist takes one look at the results: "Classic overfitting. Your training recall is 95% but test is 51%. That gap is your problem. Let me show you the dartboard analogy."

Imagine throwing darts at a target. High bias, low variance: darts cluster tightly but away from the bullseye — underfitting. Low bias, high variance: darts scatter around the bullseye — overfitting. The sweet spot is tight clusters on the bullseye.

ScenarioBiasVarianceSymptomFix
UnderfittingHighLowPoor on train AND testMore features, complex model
Sweet spotLowLowGood on bothKeep it
OverfittingLowHighGreat train, poor testRegularize, more data, simplify

L1 (Lasso) adds sum of |coefficients| — drives some to exactly zero (feature selection). L2 (Ridge) adds sum of coefficients² — shrinks all toward zero (stability). Elastic Net combines both.

from sklearn.model_selection import cross_val_score
from sklearn.ensemble import RandomForestClassifier

model = RandomForestClassifier(
    n_estimators=200,
    max_depth=10,        # limit depth to reduce variance
    min_samples_leaf=20, # require more samples per leaf
    random_state=42
)

scores = cross_val_score(model, X, y, cv=5, scoring='recall')
print(f"Mean Recall: {scores.mean():.3f} (+/- {scores.std():.3f})")
Key Insight

If performance seems "too good to be true," it almost certainly is. The most common culprit is data leakage — fitting preprocessing on the full dataset before splitting. Everything that learns from data must be fit inside the training fold only. Use Pipeline to ensure containment.

Story Resolution

Random Forest with proper 5-fold cross-validation, depth-limited trees, and a Pipeline that prevents leakage. Final recall: 73% on the holdout set. Marketing targets the top 20% risk score and offers personalized retention incentives. Three months later: churn reduced 23%, campaign spend down 40%. The Head of Marketing mentions your model in the all-hands.
