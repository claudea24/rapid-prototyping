The A/B Experiment — Statistics

New checkout flow is ready. Design the experiment and prove it works — without fooling yourself.

The product team redesigned the checkout flow from 4 steps to 2. Everyone loves it. But the VP won't ship without data: "Run an A/B test. If it works, we ship globally. If not, we scrap it." You're in charge of the experiment.

Scene 1 Setting Up the Test — Hypothesis Testing

▾
Before writing a single line of code, you need to formalize what you're testing. Your colleague says "just see if the numbers go up." That's not how experiments work. You need hypotheses, a significance level, and a clear stopping rule.

The hypothesis testing framework has six steps: (1) Formulate H&sub0; (no effect) and H&sub1; (effect exists). (2) Choose significance level α (usually 0.05). (3) Collect data. (4) Compute test statistic. (5) Compare to critical value or compute p-value. (6) Conclude: reject or fail to reject H&sub0;.

What the p-value actually means: The probability of observing data at least as extreme as what you got, assuming H&sub0; is true. It does NOT mean the probability that H&sub0; is true. It does NOT mean the probability the result is due to chance.

ErrorNameAnalogyProbability
Type IFalse PositiveConvicting an innocent personα (usually 0.05)
Type IIFalse NegativeAcquitting a guilty personβ
PowerCorrect RejectionCatching the guilty1 − β (target ≥ 0.80)

For your checkout test: H&sub0;: New flow has no effect (completion = 65%). H&sub1;: New flow increases completion (> 65%). You want to detect a 2-point lift with 80% power at α = 0.05.

Key Insight

The hardest part of A/B testing isn't the statistics — it's defining the right metric. Spend as much time debating the metric as analyzing results. "Checkout completion" sounds clear, but does it mean "clicked pay" or "payment confirmed"?

You define the metric: checkout completion rate (users reaching payment confirmation after adding an item). Now you need to figure out how many users to include and how long to run.

Scene 2 The Numbers — Confidence Intervals & Sample Size

▾
Your PM asks: "Can we just run it for a day and see?" No. You need enough samples for the result to be meaningful. And you need to understand what a confidence interval actually tells you — because your PM will ask.

A 95% confidence interval does NOT mean "there is a 95% probability the true parameter is in this interval." Correct interpretation: If we repeated this sampling procedure 100 times, approximately 95 of those intervals would contain the true parameter. Confidence is in the procedure, not the single interval.

# CI = x_bar +/- z* x (sigma / sqrt(n))
# Example: Average session duration
# n=200, mean=4.2min, SD=1.8min
SE = 1.8 / (200 ** 0.5)    # 0.127
margin = 1.96 * SE           # 0.249
CI = (4.2 - margin, 4.2 + margin)  # (3.95, 4.45)
FactorEffect on WidthCan You Control?
Sample size nLarger → narrower (via √n)Yes — run bigger study
Variability σMore → widerNo (inherent to data)
Confidence level99% wider than 95%Yes (trade-off with precision)

Sample size calculation: Current completion 65%, want to detect 2-point lift (→ 67%), α = 0.05, power = 0.80. Required: ~4,400 per arm = 8,800 total. At 2,000 checkout users/day = ~5 days needed. Round up to 14 days (two full weekly cycles + time for novelty to fade).

Key Insight

Always run at least one full weekly cycle (7+ days) to capture day-of-week effects. Weekend shopping behavior is different from weekday. And always decide the duration before looking at any results.

You lock in 14 days, 50/50 split, user-level randomization. The test launches Monday. On day 3, your PM says: "The numbers look great! Can we call it early?" You know better.

Scene 3 Don't Peek — A/B Test Pitfalls

▾
Your PM is checking the dashboard every morning. On day 5, the p-value dips below 0.05. "Ship it!" she says. You pull up the research on peeking and show her why that's a terrible idea.
PitfallWhat Goes WrongThe Fix
PeekingChecking daily and stopping when p < 0.05 inflates FP rate from 5% to ~30%Decide duration in advance. Use sequential testing if monitoring needed.
Multiple comparisonsTesting 10 metrics: ~40% chance of finding one "significant" by chanceBonferroni correction: divide α by number of tests (α = 0.05/10 = 0.005)
Novelty effectUsers engage more simply because it's new. Lift fades over time.Run long enough for novelty to wear off. Examine time trends.
Simpson's paradoxOverall positive masks negative results in every subgroupAlways segment by key dimensions (platform, geography, cohort)

Your guardrail metrics: (1) Average order value — ensure streamlined flow doesn't cause users to skip adding items. (2) 14-day return rate — ensure speed doesn't lead to accidental purchases. (3) Support contacts related to checkout — ensure it's not confusing.

Key Insight

Each peek at the data is another hypothesis test. If you check daily for 14 days, your effective false positive rate isn't 5% — it's closer to 30%. This is why pre-registration and fixed stopping rules exist.

Story Resolution

You wait the full 14 days. Final results: checkout completion up 2.3 percentage points (65.0% → 67.3%), p = 0.008, 95% CI [0.6%, 4.0%]. All guardrails pass — AOV unchanged, returns flat, support tickets actually down 12%. You present to the VP with confidence intervals, not just a p-value. She says: "Ship it." The new checkout rolls out globally the following Monday.
