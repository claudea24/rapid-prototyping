The Board Meeting — Case Studies

Revenue is up but rides are down. The board wants answers. Structure your thinking, decompose the numbers, and present with confidence.

It's 3 PM Friday. Your director walks over: "Board meeting is Monday. Revenue is up 20% but rides are down 5%. The CEO wants a clear explanation and a recommendation. I need your analysis by Sunday night." You have 48 hours.

Scene 1 The Framework — AARRR & North Star

▾
Before diving into the numbers, you need a framework. Where does "revenue" sit in the user lifecycle? Which metrics are upstream? The AARRR framework gives you the map. And you need to know what the company's North Star metric is — because that's what the board really cares about.

Key Insight

A good North Star aligns user value with business value. If it grows while users are unhappy or the business is losing money, it's the wrong metric. For a rideshare company, the North Star is likely "completed rides" — but the board is seeing revenue up and rides down. Something is off.

You identify the tension: rides (the North Star) are down, but revenue is up. That means revenue per ride must have increased significantly. Time to decompose the numbers.

Scene 2 Diagnosing the Numbers — Metric Decomposition

▾
Revenue = Rides × Revenue per Ride. If revenue is up 20% and rides are down 5%, then revenue per ride must be up ~26%. But why? You need to segment, hypothesize, and investigate. 
# Revenue decomposition
Revenue = Rides × Revenue_per_Ride
1.20 = 0.95 × X
X = 1.20 / 0.95 ≈ 1.263
# Revenue per ride up ~26%

# Investigation framework:
# 1. Is it a data problem? (logging, schema migration)
# 2. External factor? (holiday, competitor, weather)
# 3. Internal change? (pricing, feature, experiment)
# 4. Segment to isolate (platform, geo, cohort, ride type)
# 5. Quantify the contribution

Your hypotheses: (a) Price increase — did the company raise base fares or implement more aggressive surge pricing? (b) Mix shift — fewer short commutes but steady long/airport rides, raising the average fare. (c) Product mix — launch of premium tier (Black/XL) capturing a larger share of expensive rides.

The six-step framework: (1) Clarify — restate the problem, ask scope questions. (2) Define Metrics — primary metric + guardrails. (3) Hypothesize — before touching data. (4) Analyze — segment along relevant dimensions. (5) Recommend — concrete action + expected impact. (6) Measure — how to validate.

Key Insight

Always check for data problems first. A schema migration breaking the ride tracking? Embarrassingly common. Rule it out before building elaborate theories. Then check external factors. Only then investigate internal changes.

Segmentation reveals the answer: a new surge pricing algorithm launched 6 weeks ago. It's generating more revenue per ride but suppressing demand for price-sensitive short trips. You need to size the opportunity and recommend a path forward.

Scene 3 The Recommendation — Sizing & Experiment Design

▾
The board will ask: "How big is the opportunity if we fix this?" You need a market sizing estimate. And they'll want to know: "How do we validate the fix?" You need an experiment design. Lead with the answer, then show your work.

Your sizing: Pre-surge, short rides were 40% of volume (~2M rides/month). Post-surge, they dropped 25% to ~1.5M. Lost rides: 500K/month. At $8 average fare: $4M/month in lost revenue. But surge generates extra $6M/month on remaining rides. Net: +$2M/month. The question is whether the ride volume decline will accelerate.

Communicate using the Pyramid Principle: Lead with the answer. "The revenue increase is driven by a new surge pricing algorithm. It generates +$2M/month net, but ride volume is declining and may accelerate. I recommend A/B testing a modified algorithm that caps surge at 2x for trips under 3 miles." Then show the supporting analysis.

Key Insight

The best technical answer, poorly communicated, loses to a decent answer well communicated. State the conclusion first. Quantify everything — not "affected a lot of users" but "affected approximately 500,000 rides, roughly 25% of short-trip volume." Name trade-offs proactively.

Story Resolution

You present Monday morning: "Revenue is up because surge pricing is working. Rides are down because it's suppressing price-sensitive demand. Net positive today, but the trend is concerning. I recommend capping surge for short trips and running a 3-week A/B test to validate." The CEO nods. The board approves the experiment. Three weeks later, the modified algorithm recovers 60% of lost rides while retaining 85% of the revenue uplift. Your director mentions you for the next promotion cycle.
