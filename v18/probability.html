Probability Mock Interview

4 questions with follow-ups · ~10 minutes

Q1
A medical test is 99% accurate (both sensitivity and specificity). The disease prevalence is 1%. You test positive. What's the probability you actually have the disease?
A) 99% — the test is 99% accurate, so it's almost certain.
B) About 50% — by Bayes' theorem, the low base rate drags it way down.
C) Somewhere between 50-99% — high accuracy but low prevalence matters.
↪
Walk me through the math. Why do base rates matter so much here?
❌ Mediocre Answer

"Because the disease is rare, there are more false positives. Something about Bayes' theorem."

✅ Great Answer

"Out of 10,000 people: 100 have the disease (1%), 9,900 don't. Of the 100 sick: 99 test positive (true positives). Of the 9,900 healthy: 99 test positive (false positives). So 99 true positives out of 198 total positives = 50%. The base rate matters because when the disease is rare, even a small false positive rate generates many false alarms from the huge healthy population. This is base rate neglect — one of the most important biases in data science, affecting everything from fraud detection to spam filtering."

Q2
The Monty Hall problem: you pick a door, the host opens another revealing a goat. Should you switch?
A) No — it's 50/50 now. Two doors, one car. Doesn't matter.
B) Yes, always switch — switching gives you 2/3 chance of winning.
C) Yes, but only slightly better. The math is complicated.
↪
WHY is it 2/3 and not 1/2? Most people get this wrong — convince me.
❌ Mediocre Answer

"Because the host gives you information by opening a door. So switching is better."

✅ Great Answer

"Your initial pick has a 1/3 chance of being correct — that never changes. The other two doors together have 2/3. When the host (who KNOWS where the car is) opens a goat door, that 2/3 probability concentrates onto the remaining door. The key insight: the host's action is NOT random — he MUST open a goat door. If you picked wrong initially (2/3 probability), switching ALWAYS wins. If you picked right initially (1/3 probability), switching ALWAYS loses. So switching wins 2/3 of the time. Think of it with 100 doors — you pick one, host opens 98 goat doors. Would you switch? Of course."

Q3
What's the expected value of rolling a fair six-sided die?
A) 3 — it's the middle number.
B) 3.5 — the average of 1+2+3+4+5+6 divided by 6.
C) Between 3 and 4 — each face has equal probability.
↪
Now a game: I charge you $4 to play, and you win whatever the die shows. Should you play? What if I offer best of 2 rolls?
❌ Mediocre Answer

"No, because 3.5 < 4. With two rolls, maybe it's worth it."

✅ Great Answer

"Single roll: E[winnings] = $3.50, cost = $4, so expected loss of $0.50 per game — don't play. Best of 2 rolls: E[max(X1, X2)] = 4.47 (calculated by summing over all 36 outcomes). Since 4.47 > 4, the game now has positive expected value of $0.47 per play. You should play the two-roll version. The general principle: whenever evaluating a bet, compute E[payoff] - cost. Positive = take it (in the long run). This is the foundation of decision theory."

Q4
The birthday problem: how many people do you need in a room for a 50% chance that two share a birthday?
A) About 183 — half of 365.
B) Just 23 — it's surprisingly low because you're checking all PAIRS.
C) Around 50-60 — fewer than you'd think due to combinatorics.
↪
Walk me through the math. Why does complement counting work well here?
❌ Mediocre Answer

"There are lots of pairs so the probability adds up. Complement counting avoids double-counting."

✅ Great Answer

"Instead of computing P(at least one match) directly (messy, many overlapping cases), we compute P(NO matches) and subtract from 1. Person 1 has any birthday: 365/365. Person 2 must differ: 364/365. Person 3: 363/365. And so on. P(no match among 23) = (365/365)(364/365)(363/365)...(343/365) ≈ 0.493. So P(match) ≈ 0.507 > 50%. It's counterintuitive because with 23 people there are C(23,2) = 253 pairs being checked — people think linearly (23 vs 365) but the comparison space grows quadratically. Complement counting is the go-to technique whenever 'at least one' appears in a probability question."
