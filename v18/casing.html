Case Study Mock Interview

4 questions with follow-ups · ~10 minutes

Q1
DAU dropped 15% yesterday. Walk me through your investigation.
A) Check if there's a bug in the product. If not, it's probably seasonality.
B) First: is the data correct? Check logging, ETL pipelines, definition changes. Then segment: by platform, country, new vs returning, acquisition channel. Look for external events (holidays, outages, competitor launches).
C) Segment by platform and user type to narrow down where the drop happened.
↪
Good instinct to check data first. What data problems would you look for specifically?
❌ Mediocre Answer

"Check if the data pipeline ran successfully and the numbers look reasonable."

✅ Great Answer

"Specific checks: (1) Pipeline freshness — did the ETL job complete? Check for partial loads (row counts vs previous days). (2) Definition changes — did someone modify the DAU definition or filter criteria? (3) Logging issues — check if a tracking SDK update dropped events on a platform (common with mobile app deploys). (4) Bot filtering changes — did we start or stop filtering bot traffic? (5) Timezone/calendar — did DST shift or a deploy change the UTC cutoff? Only after ruling these out do I look at real product/behavioral causes. In my experience, ~30% of 'metric drops' are data issues."

Q2
Revenue is up 20% but rides are down 5%. How do you explain this?
A) People are taking fewer but more expensive rides. Simple.
B) Decompose: Revenue = Rides x Revenue/Ride. If rides are down but revenue is up, revenue per ride increased significantly. Then ask WHY — price increases? Surge pricing? Shift to longer/premium trips? Mix shift?
C) The mix of ride types must have changed — more premium rides.
↪
Tell me more about metric decomposition. How do you structure this systematically?
❌ Mediocre Answer

"Break revenue into components and see which one changed."

✅ Great Answer

"Systematic decomposition: Revenue = Volume x Price x Mix. For ride-sharing: Revenue = Total Rides x Avg Revenue per Ride. Then deeper: Avg Revenue/Ride = Base fare + Distance component + Surge multiplier. Segment by: ride type (UberX vs Black), geography (city-level), time (peak vs off-peak). A 'mix shift' means the composition changed — e.g., losing 10% of cheap short rides while maintaining premium rides mechanically increases average revenue. Always decompose multiplicatively first, then segment additively to find WHICH slice drives the change."

Q3
Estimate the number of piano tuners in Chicago.
A) Maybe a few hundred? I'd Google it to be sure.
B) Start from population, estimate pianos per household, tunings per year, time per tuning, and work backwards to how many tuners the demand supports.
C) Chicago has ~3 million people, so maybe 1 tuner per 10,000 people = 300 tuners.
↪
Walk through the math. What assumptions are you making, and which ones matter most?
❌ Mediocre Answer

"3M people, maybe 5% have pianos, tuned once a year... so about 150,000 tunings. A tuner does maybe 1,000 a year. So ~150 tuners."

✅ Great Answer

"Chicago metro: ~2.7M people, ~1M households. ~5% own pianos = 50,000 pianos. Add institutions (schools, churches, concert halls) ~5,000 more. Total: ~55,000 pianos. Each tuned ~1.5x/year = 82,500 tunings/year. Each tuning takes ~2 hours (including travel). A tuner works ~250 days/year x 4 tunings/day = 1,000 tunings/year. 82,500 / 1,000 = ~83 piano tuners. The assumptions that matter most: % of households with pianos and tunings per year. A 2x error in piano ownership changes the answer more than any other variable. The real answer is reportedly ~100, so this is close."

Q4
How would you define a north star metric for Airbnb?
A) Revenue — it's the ultimate measure of success for any company.
B) "Nights booked" — it captures both guest demand and host supply, correlates with revenue, and reflects the core value proposition of the product.
C) Monthly active users — more users means more bookings means more revenue.
↪
How do you ensure a north star metric aligns the whole organization and doesn't cause perverse incentives?
❌ Mediocre Answer

"Pick one metric everyone tracks and optimize for it."

✅ Great Answer

"A good north star metric has 4 properties: (1) Reflects value — 'nights booked' means guests found a place and hosts earned money. (2) Leading indicator — it moves before revenue does, giving early signal. (3) Actionable — teams can influence it (unlike pure revenue which depends on pricing). (4) Hard to game. To prevent perverse incentives, pair it with guardrail metrics: cancellation rate (ensures quality bookings), host NPS (ensures host satisfaction), and review scores (ensures guest experience). If nights booked goes up but cancellation rate spikes, something is wrong. The north star focuses energy; guardrails prevent dysfunction."
