Stats Mock Interview

4 questions with follow-ups · ~10 minutes

Q1
What is a p-value? Explain it simply.
A) The probability that the null hypothesis is true.
B) The probability of observing data this extreme (or more) assuming the null hypothesis IS true.
C) A measure of how significant the result is — lower means more significant.
↪
Good. What's the most common misconception about p-values?
❌ Mediocre Answer

"People think p=0.05 means there's a 5% chance the result is wrong."

✅ Great Answer

"The #1 misconception: 'the p-value is the probability the null hypothesis is true.' It's NOT. It's P(data | H0), not P(H0 | data). A p-value of 0.03 means: IF the null is true, there's a 3% chance of seeing data this extreme. It says nothing about the probability of H0 itself. Also: p < 0.05 doesn't mean the effect is large or important — with enough data, even trivially small effects become 'significant.' Always pair p-values with effect sizes and confidence intervals."

Q2
Design an A/B test for a new checkout flow. How do you set it up?
A) Show the new flow to everyone for a week, then compare to last week's data.
B) Randomly split users 50/50, define a primary metric (conversion rate), calculate required sample size, run until you hit it, then analyze.
C) Run both versions simultaneously and pick whichever has higher conversion.
↪
How do you determine the required sample size?
❌ Mediocre Answer

"Just run it for a week or two and see if the results are significant."

✅ Great Answer

"Sample size depends on four inputs: (1) Baseline conversion rate (e.g., 5%). (2) Minimum detectable effect (MDE) — the smallest improvement worth detecting (e.g., 0.5% absolute lift). (3) Significance level (alpha) — typically 0.05. (4) Power (1 - beta) — typically 0.80. Plug into a power calculator. Smaller MDE = much larger sample needed. This is why you decide sample size BEFORE running the test — it prevents 'peeking' and stopping early when results look good by chance."

Q3
Results show p=0.03 but the test only ran for 2 days. Should we ship it?
A) Yes! p < 0.05 means it's statistically significant. Ship it.
B) No — 2 days likely isn't enough. We probably haven't hit the pre-calculated sample size, and early results are unreliable due to peeking bias.
C) Maybe — if the effect is really large, 2 days could be enough.
↪
Tell me more about the peeking problem. Why is checking results early dangerous?
❌ Mediocre Answer

"Looking early is fine as long as you wait for significance. If it's p < 0.05, you can stop."

✅ Great Answer

"Peeking inflates false positive rates dramatically. If you check significance after every batch, the chance of seeing p < 0.05 by luck can exceed 30% even with no real effect. Why? Each peek is another 'draw' — more draws = more chances for a false positive. Solutions: (1) Fixed horizon — commit to sample size, analyze once. (2) Sequential testing (e.g., group sequential design) — allows predefined checkpoints with adjusted thresholds. (3) Bayesian approach — continuously update posterior probability. Also watch for day-of-week effects — 2 days misses weekend behavior."

Q4
Explain the Central Limit Theorem.
A) Large enough samples will always be normally distributed.
B) The distribution of SAMPLE MEANS approaches a normal distribution as sample size increases, regardless of the underlying population distribution.
C) The more data you collect, the more your data becomes normal.
↪
Why does the CLT matter for data science in practice?
❌ Mediocre Answer

"It matters because normal distributions are easy to work with."

✅ Great Answer

"The CLT is the foundation of nearly all inferential statistics we use: (1) Confidence intervals — we can build CIs for means even when the underlying data isn't normal. (2) A/B tests — t-tests and z-tests assume the sampling distribution of the mean is normal, which CLT guarantees for n ≥ 30ish. (3) Survey estimation — we can estimate population means from samples. The key nuance: CLT applies to the distribution of the sample MEAN, not the data itself. Your data can be wildly skewed, but the mean of 50 samples from it will be approximately normal."
