Concept Cards

Fundamentals

Common
  • Addition Rule: P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B)
  • Multiplication Rule: P(A and B) = P(A) · P(B|A)
  • Independence: Events A and B are independent if P(A and B) = P(A) · P(B)
  • Complement: P(A') = 1 − P(A)
  • Permutations: n! / (n−r)! — order matters
  • Combinations: n! / [r!(n−r)!] — order does not matter

Bayes' Theorem

Legendary
Bayes' Theorem P(A|B) = P(B|A) · P(A) / P(B)
Intuition Prior × Likelihood / Evidence = Posterior

Bayes' Theorem lets you update your beliefs given new evidence. It is central to countless DS interview questions.

Classic disease test question: A test is 99% sensitive and 99% specific. If disease prevalence is 1%, what is the probability a positive result is truly positive? Using Bayes: P(Disease|+) = (0.99 × 0.01) / [(0.99 × 0.01) + (0.01 × 0.99)] = 50%. The low prior dramatically reduces the posterior despite high test accuracy.

Key Distributions

Rare
  • Bernoulli: Single trial, success probability p. E[X] = p.
  • Binomial: n independent Bernoulli trials. E[X] = np, Var = np(1−p).
  • Poisson: Count of events in fixed interval. E[X] = Var = λ.
  • Geometric: Trials until first success. E[X] = 1/p.
  • Normal (Gaussian): Bell curve. Defined by μ and σ². The 68-95-99.7 rule.
  • Exponential: Time between Poisson events. E[X] = 1/λ. Memoryless property.
  • Uniform: Equal probability across [a, b]. E[X] = (a+b)/2.

Expected Value & Variance

Rare
Expected Value E[X] = ∑ x·P(X=x)
Variance Var(X) = E[X²] − (E[X])²

Linearity of Expectation:

  • E[aX + b] = a·E[X] + b
  • E[X + Y] = E[X] + E[Y] — always true, even without independence
  • Var(aX + b) = a²·Var(X)
  • If X, Y independent: Var(X + Y) = Var(X) + Var(Y)

Law of Large Numbers

Common

As sample size n increases, the sample mean converges to the population mean. In other words, more data gives you a more accurate estimate of the true value.

A/B Testing Connection: The LLN is why we need sufficient sample sizes in A/B tests. Small samples can show misleading results. As n grows, the observed conversion rate stabilizes around the true rate, making our test conclusions reliable. This is the foundation for power analysis and minimum detectable effect calculations.

Classic Interview Puzzles

Legendary
  • Birthday Problem: Only 23 people needed for a >50% chance two share a birthday. The key insight: calculate the complement (no shared birthdays) and subtract from 1.
  • Monty Hall Problem: Always switch doors. Staying gives 1/3, switching gives 2/3. The host's reveal of a goat provides information that shifts the probability.
  • Coupon Collector: Expected draws to collect all n unique coupons is n·H(n) ≈ n·ln(n). Each new coupon gets progressively harder to find.
  • Dice Problems: Expected rolls to see all 6 faces = 6·(1 + 1/2 + 1/3 + 1/4 + 1/5 + 1/6) ≈ 14.7. A direct application of the coupon collector framework.
